# Saddle Position — Fitter Corpus

**Maturity: foundation** (L1 core complete, and unusually well-grounded for a bootstrap: the
saddle-height methods are carried with Tier-A systematic-review evidence, and the KOPS dispute is
sourced on both sides. No L2 per-discipline differential and no L3 force/kinematics depth — see
Gaps). Read [`INDEX.md`](INDEX.md) first (corpus rules, tiers, ⚠ GUIDANCE-NOT-PRESCRIPTION,
⚠ DISPUTED, the ID-range rule).

Fact IDs are stable and append-only (`SDL-n`). Every fact carries its **tier** and a
**Couplings** line where the dimension moves something else.

> **⚠ GUIDANCE-NOT-PRESCRIPTION applies with full force to this chapter.** Every method below is
> presented as *a named method with a stated derivation and a known disagreement*, never as the
> correct number. Saddle height is the fit dimension with the most competing published formulas
> and the most literature showing they disagree with each other; a corpus that merged them into
> one figure would be manufacturing a certainty the evidence does not support.

## Saddle height — the methods, side by side

**SDL-1 — There is no established single method for setting saddle height; the published methods
fall into two families, and they demonstrably disagree.** A narrative review (Bini, Hume & Croft
2011, 62 references) concludes that methods "are varied and not well established," and sorts them
into (a) **proportion-of-limb-length methods** — Hamley & Thomas, trochanteric length, ischial-
tuberosity-to-floor, LeMond, and the heel method — and (b) **knee-flexion-angle methods**, which
set height by a target joint angle rather than a body ratio. A 2021 systematic review by the same
lead author (Bini & Priego-Quesada), screening 29,398 records to 41 included studies, updates the
conclusion in a specific direction: **strong evidence supports configuring saddle height by
DYNAMIC measurement of the knee angle** (i.e. while actually pedalling), and limb kinematics do
change with saddle height.
- *Tier:* A. Sources: Bini, Hume & Croft, *Sports Med* 2011;41(6):463–76
  ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.2165/11588740-000000000-00000)); Bini & Priego-Quesada, *J Sports Sci*
  2021;40(4):386–400 ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/02640414.2021.1994727)). Retrieved via PubMed.

**SDL-2 — The proportion-of-inseam methods and the knee-angle methods can be set against each
other, and when they are, they disagree for a majority of riders.** Ferrer-Roca et al. (2012)
measured well-trained cyclists and found **56.5% were outside the commonly recommended 106–109%
of inseam range**, and 26% were outside an appropriate knee-flexion angle by dynamic measurement.
Their explicit conclusion is that setting saddle height to 106–109% of inseam **may not ensure**
a knee flexion of 30–40°. They proposed a regression relating saddle height, inseam and knee
angle, outputting a revised **108.6–110.4% of inseam**.
- *Couplings:* the two families are not interchangeable — an inseam formula is a *prediction* of
  the knee angle, and the prediction has measurable error. If a rider is set by formula and then
  measured dynamically, expect a discrepancy; the literature says trust the measurement.
- *Tier:* A. Source: Ferrer-Roca, Roig, Galilea & García-López, *J Strength Cond Res*
  2012;26(11):3025–9 ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1519/JSC.0b013e318245c09d)). Retrieved via PubMed.

**SDL-3 — The knee-angle target itself depends on HOW and WHEN it is measured, and the three
common protocols produce different numbers for the same rider.** Millour et al. compared three
methodologies on 26 cyclists using 2D video: the **Holmes method** (25–35° knee angle, measured
*statically* with the pedal at bottom dead centre); a **dynamic** target of 30–40° at maximum knee
extension while pedalling; and dynamic measurement at bottom dead centre. Findings: the dynamic
knee angle at BDC was **8° greater** than the static angle at BDC, and **3° greater** than the
dynamic angle at maximum knee extension. They propose a new reference range of **33–43° at bottom
dead centre during pedalling**, and state the purpose plainly — to stop fitters mixing a target
from one protocol with a measurement from another.
- *Couplings:* this is a **measurement-protocol coupling**, and it is the most commonly botched
  one in practice: quoting "25–35°" (a static Holmes number) while measuring a rider dynamically
  builds an ~8° error straight into the fit. **Always state which protocol a knee-angle number
  belongs to.**
- *Tier:* A. Source: Millour, Duc, Puel & Bertucci, *Acta Bioeng Biomech* 2019;21(4):93–99.
  Retrieved via PubMed (PMID 32022807).

**SDL-4 — The named proportion methods, recorded with what each is derived from.** Presented as a
list of documented methods, per ⚠ GUIDANCE-NOT-PRESCRIPTION — **not** as options to average.
- **Hamley & Thomas** — saddle height as a percentage of inseam (the 106–109% band tested in
  SDL-2 descends from this family).
- **LeMond** — inseam × 0.883, measured BB centre to saddle top. Named in both Bini reviews and
  tested directly as a protocol by Encarnación-Martínez et al. (SDL-5).
- **Trochanteric-length** and **ischial-tuberosity-to-floor** methods — proportion methods using a
  different bony landmark than crotch-to-floor inseam.
- **Heel method** — set height so the leg is fully straight with the *heel* on the pedal at BDC;
  moving to the ball of the foot then yields a knee bend. Reported by practitioner sources as a
  sanity check yielding roughly 25–30° of knee bend, which places it in the Holmes *static* band
  (SDL-3) — so it inherits that protocol's caveat.
- **An MTB-adapted inseam formula** circulating in practitioner writing: inseam × 0.875, then
  **minus 5–10 mm for MTB** (see DSC-2 for the discipline rationale). Recorded as Tier B, not A —
  no peer-reviewed source for the 0.875 coefficient or the MTB subtraction was located this round.
- *Tier:* A for the method names and the 106–109%/0.883 figures (Bini 2011, Bini & Priego-Quesada
  2021, Ferrer-Roca 2012, Encarnación-Martínez 2021 — all above/below); **B** for the heel-method
  procedure and the 0.875-minus-5-10 mm variant. Sources: as SDL-1/SDL-2/SDL-5; Roadman Cycling
  *MTB Bike Fit Basics* (roadmancycling.com/blog/mtb-bike-fit-basics) for the practitioner variant.

**SDL-5 — The proportion formulas were derived predominantly on male cyclists and measurably fail
female riders.** Encarnación-Martínez et al. compared four saddle-height protocols (preferred,
Ferrer-Roca, LeMond & Guimard, static goniometry) across 15 men and 15 women. **Only 20–33% of
the women** ended within the recommended knee-extension range under each formula-based protocol —
against **47–60% of the men**, itself only "moderately suitable." The one protocol that worked for
women was their own **preferred** (self-selected) height, at 73%. The authors attribute the gap
partly to anatomical differences and partly to the equations having been obtained primarily from
male cyclists, and call for research on female cyclists. They offer a multiple regression using
inseam, stature, foot length and knee angle (R² = 0.917).
- ⚠ This is a **sourced equity gap in the domain's own evidence base**, not an editorial aside.
  Any fit guidance this project ever surfaces that leans on an inseam coefficient must carry it.
- *Couplings:* it also partly explains SDL-2's 56.5% out-of-range finding — a formula validated on
  one population applied to another produces exactly that kind of miss rate.
- *Tier:* A. Source: Encarnación-Martínez, Ferrer-Roca & García-López, *J Strength Cond Res*
  2021;35(2):519–526 ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1519/JSC.0000000000002689)). Retrieved via PubMed.

**SDL-6 — Height alone is a weak input; limb length, flexibility and training history predict
freely-chosen position better.** Holliday & Swart regressed the four main bike-configuration
outputs (saddle height, saddle setback, handlebar reach, handlebar drop) on anthropometrics,
flexibility and training history across 50 well-trained male cyclists. **Trochanteric leg length,
the Knee Extension Angle test, and the modified Schober test were significant predictors of
saddle height**; hamstring flexibility predicted handlebar drop. Observed joint ranges: knee
36°±7°, elbow 19°±8°, and they argue hip (77°±5°) and shoulder (112°±7°) should be treated as true
clinical joint angles.
- ⚠ This is the Tier-A basis for the corpus's scepticism of **any single-variable height formula**
  — including SIZ-9's RideLogic ×2.5 shortcut, which is flagged DISPUTED partly on this evidence.
- *Couplings:* flexibility → sustainable bar drop is a *rider* variable feeding a *bike* number;
  it is the cleanest documented case of fit being a property of the pair, not the bike.
- *Tier:* A. Source: Holliday & Swart, *Sports Med Health Sci* 2021;3(2):93–100
  ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smhs.2021.02.007)); PMC9219349. Retrieved via PubMed.

**SDL-7 — How much saddle-height change actually matters: below ~4% of leg length, effects are
small; around 5%, joint kinematics move a lot.** The 2011 review reports that changes of **less
than 4% of trochanteric length do not seem to affect injury risk or performance**, while the
majority of evidence indicated a **5% change in saddle height altered knee-joint kinematics by
~35% and moments by ~16%**. The 2021 systematic review restates the low end with moderate
evidence: changes **under 4% of leg length** produce trivial-to-small changes in lower-limb loads
and **no** effect on oxygen uptake or efficiency. Patellofemoral compressive force appears
inversely related to saddle height (i.e. a *lower* saddle raises it); tibiofemoral force effects
are uncertain. Both reviews note the evidence on supramaximal performance and on injury risk
specifically is **limited**.
- ⚠ **The honest reading, and the one to give a rider:** this is why fit is iterative rather than
  exact. There is a band within which precision is not doing anything measurable, and a magnitude
  beyond which it clearly is. It is also why "my saddle is 3 mm off" is rarely the real problem.
- *Couplings:* SDL-9 — a fore/aft change silently changes effective height, so a setback
  adjustment can push a rider across this threshold without the seatpost moving.
- *Tier:* A. Sources: Bini, Hume & Croft 2011 and Bini & Priego-Quesada 2021 (as SDL-1).

**SDL-8 — Overuse knee pain is common enough in cyclists that saddle height is treated as an
injury variable, not only a performance one.** The 2011 review states the occurrence of overuse
knee-joint pain in cyclists at **50%** and, weighing the conflicting evidence, offers a
conservative recommendation: **set by the knee-flexion-angle method (25–30°)** to reduce knee-
injury risk and minimise oxygen uptake. Note this recommendation predates the 2021 review's
stronger steer toward *dynamic* measurement (SDL-1) and uses a *static-band* number (SDL-3) —
carry both, and carry the protocol label.
- *Tier:* A. Source: Bini, Hume & Croft 2011 (as SDL-1).

## ⚠ DISPUTED — saddle setback and KOPS

**SDL-9 — Saddle fore/aft (setback) is the horizontal distance from the BB to the saddle, and it
is primarily a WEIGHT-DISTRIBUTION adjustment — not a reach adjustment.** Moving the saddle back
increases setback and shifts weight rearward; forward shifts it onto the front wheel and the
hands. Fore/aft also changes muscle recruitment (the relative contribution of quads, hamstrings
and glutes) and hip angle — forward opens the hip angle, back closes it. Fitters are emphatic
about one misuse: **do not move the saddle to fix reach.** If reach is wrong and the saddle is
right, change the stem or bar, because moving the saddle changes the pedalling relationship it was
set for.
- *Couplings — the most important in the chapter:* **moving the saddle AFT effectively RAISES
  saddle height; moving it FORE effectively LOWERS it** (the saddle travels along the seat-tube
  angle, not horizontally). Any significant fore/aft change requires re-checking height. Setback
  also changes handlebar reach as a side effect — which is exactly why it must not be used *as* a
  reach adjustment.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Sources: BikeRadar *How to set saddle setback on your bike*
  (bikeradar.com/advice/sizing-and-fit/saddle-setback), quoting fitter commentary on weight
  distribution and the do-not-fix-reach rule; Peloton Physio *Saddle position*
  (pelotonphysio.com.au/blog/saddle-position) on muscle recruitment and hip angle; myvelofit
  *Bike Fit Adjustments: The right order* (myvelofit.com/fit-academy/bike-fitting-order-of-
  adjustments/) on the fore/aft↔height interaction.

**SDL-10 ⚠ DISPUTED — KOPS is simultaneously the most widely taught setback starting point and
the most prominently attacked one. This is the corpus's canonical dispute; present BOTH sides,
always.**

- **Position 1 — KOPS as the standard starting point (widely published, including by makers).**
  KOPS ("Knee Over Pedal Spindle") sets setback so that with the cranks horizontal and the ball
  of the foot over the pedal axle, a plumb line from the knee intersects the pedal axle. Giant
  publishes it as its own procedure with a correction rule: if the kneecap falls **behind** the
  axle move the saddle **forward**; if **ahead**, move it **back** — and explicitly calls the
  result *a starting point* to be adjusted by preference. A fitting physio lists KOPS alongside a
  Balance Test and the rider's current position as viable starting points. *Tier: A-mfr + B.*
  Sources: Giant *How to adjust the saddle on your bike*
  (giant-bicycles.com/us/adjusting-your-saddle); Peloton Physio (as SDL-9).
- **Position 2 — KOPS has no physiological foundation (named authorities, in writing).** Keith
  Bontrager's essay *The Myth of K.O.P.S.*, edited and hosted by Sheldon Brown, argues from
  anatomy, dynamics and custom-framebuilding experience that there is **no physiological basis**
  for aligning the knee over the pedal spindle, and offers an alternative approach considering the
  rider in all riding positions rather than only the seated one; the editor's preface frames it
  explicitly as myth-exploding. Steve Hogg makes two distinct attacks: first that the rule is
  **misnamed** — what is actually meant is the *tibial tuberosity* over the pedal axle (TTOPS),
  not the knee — and second that it "has achieved validity by repetition," holding its status
  only in the absence of a contending view. His alternative criterion is the **minimum setback
  that lets the rider cantilever their torso from the pelvis without excessive upper-body support**
  — a self-supporting position — so that effort goes into pedalling rather than holding the torso
  up. *Tier: B.* Sources: Bontrager (ed. Roosa/Brown/Allen), *The Myth of K.O.P.S.*
  (sheldonbrown.com/kops.html); Steve Hogg, *Seat set back: for road bikes*
  (stevehoggbikefitting.com/bikefit/2011/05/seat-set-back-for-road-bikes/).

**What the two sides actually agree on — state this whenever the dispute is raised.** Neither side
claims KOPS is a *destination*; Giant calls it a starting point and Hogg's objection is to its
treatment as a *law*. The substantive disagreement is whether it is a **useful** starting point or
an **arbitrary** one that has survived by repetition. Note also that both critics are writing
about **road** position; no source located this round argues KOPS either way specifically for MTB,
where the rider is frequently not seated at all (see DSC-1) and MTB seatposts are commonly
zero-offset, limiting rearward adjustment (SDL-11). **The corpus declares no winner.**

**SDL-11 — Available setback range is hardware-limited, and MTB is the most limited case.** MTB
seatposts — dropper posts especially — are typically **zero-offset**, with the head in line with
the shaft, so the saddle can go far forward but not far back; road and gravel riders can choose
between zero-offset and setback posts (commonly ~15 mm, sometimes adjustable 15–35 mm). MTB
saddles also tend to have shorter rails, further narrowing the range. A fitter flags this as a
**growing** problem because MTB seat-tube angles continue to steepen, pushing riders into an
upright position that suits grinding uphill but not racing uphill or hip-hinged power production.
Short-nose saddles compound it with shorter rails still.
- ⚠ **CONTRADICTION CANDIDATE — flagged, not acted on (INDEX rule 6, boundary rule).** The catalog
  models `dropper` with `diameter` and `drop`, and rigid `seatpost` with `diameter`, but **carries
  no seatpost setback/offset field** and no saddle rail-length field. That is correct and harmless
  for a *compatibility* engine — offset has no bearing on whether a post fits a seat tube, so
  **no engine rule is wrong**. It is recorded here only because a future *fit-advice* surface
  could not answer a setback question from the current schema. **For the coordinator to triage;
  no rule change is proposed and none is warranted on compatibility grounds.**
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Sources: BikeRadar (as SDL-9) on post offsets and short-nose rails; John
  Higgins / FitKit Systems (fitkitsystems.com/how-to-fit-a-mountain-bike-practical-techniques-and-
  essential-adjustments/) on the MTB-specific squeeze and steepening seat angles.

## Saddle tilt

**SDL-12 — Start level, and treat a large tilt as a symptom of something else being wrong.**
Consensus procedure: set the saddle level *first*, then set height and fore/aft, then revisit
tilt last. "Level" means the part actually sat on — for most saddles the **middle third** — is
level, checked with a spirit level on level ground. A rider currently running a large tilt in
either direction is likely compensating for another fit error, so levelling is a reset, not just
a step. Adjustments are small: **1° is often enough**, and it would be very uncommon to need more
than 1° nose-up.
- *Couplings:* tilt sits *inside* the ordering rule — height and fore/aft are set against a level
  reference, so changing tilt afterwards invalidates them if it is large.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Sources: myvelofit *Saddle Tilt 101*
  (myvelofit.com/fit-academy/saddle-tilt-101/) and *Bike Fit Adjustments: The right order*
  (as SDL-9); Giant (as SDL-10) — "start by setting your saddle with a level nose," and the
  explicit statement that there is **no golden rule** for angle.

**SDL-13 — Tilt is direction-aware, and each direction has a distinct documented failure mode.**
- **Nose UP** encourages posterior pelvic rotation — the nose blocks the pelvis rolling forward —
  producing increased spinal flexion and possible lower-back pain, over-reaching for the bars
  (hence neck and shoulder pain), inhibited glutes, and **increased perineal pressure at the
  front**. A fitting physio states there are very few occasions where a saddle should be nose-up,
  "in fact almost never."
- **Nose DOWN excessively** makes the rider slide forward, so they sit on the narrow part of the
  saddle (less pelvic support, *more* perineal pressure — the opposite of the intent), brace
  against sliding with the quads (loading the knees), and **shift weight onto the hands**, causing
  hand and wrist pain.
- **The correction rules**: pressure at the front of the saddle → nose down *a little* (1°),
  repeat as needed; sliding forward constantly → nose up *a little*.
- ⚠ **The most important entry in this section:** riders commonly tilt the nose down excessively
  to chase perineal numbness. Both sources say the same thing — that is the wrong fix, and it
  makes the pressure worse by moving the rider onto the narrow section. The indicated moves are a
  **different saddle** (often wider, or with a cutout) and/or **lowering the saddle**. Cross-
  reference `fit-problems.md` PRB-2, which carries the mandatory professional-referral line for
  any perineal or numbness symptom.
- *Couplings:* tilt ↔ hand load ↔ perineal pressure is a three-way trade; the wrong tilt moves
  pain from one contact point to another rather than removing it.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Sources: Peloton Physio (as SDL-9) — the nose-up mechanism and the
  "almost never" claim; myvelofit *Saddle Tilt 101* (as SDL-12) — the 1° correction rules and the
  wrong-fix warning.

**SDL-14 — Saddle shape changes how much tilt is appropriate, so a tilt number is not portable
between saddles.** A saddle with a wave shape or a kick-up at the rear can be tilted down
considerably more than a completely flat one before the rider starts sliding. How much down-tilt
suits a given rider also depends on riding type, flexibility, core strength and preference.
- *Tier:* B. Source: Peloton Physio (as SDL-9).

## Gaps

Honest list of what is missing to reach `professional` (L2) and beyond:

- **L2 — no MTB-specific saddle-height evidence.** Every Tier-A source in this chapter measured
  road cyclists, indoor cyclists, or laboratory ergometers. The MTB adjustment (SDL-4's
  "minus 5–10 mm", DSC-2's rationale) rests entirely on Tier-B practitioner assertion. **No study
  applying the knee-angle protocols to off-road riders was located.** This is the chapter's
  largest honesty gap and should be stated in any MTB saddle-height answer.
- **L2 — dropper posts are unaddressed as a saddle-height variable.** A dropper means the rider
  has *two* saddle heights (pedalling and descending) and the fit literature has none of this.
  Also unwritten: how dropper travel choice interacts with the seat-tube-length constraint.
- **L2 — no discipline differential sourced.** Whether XC, trail, enduro and DH warrant different
  height/setback targets is asserted in `discipline-fit.md` at Tier B only.
- **L3 — no depth on the force literature.** SDL-7 names patellofemoral compressive force and
  tibiofemoral uncertainty in one line; the underlying studies have not been read.
- **L3 — female-specific fit is a sourced gap, not yet a chapter section.** SDL-5 establishes that
  the formulas fail women; the *alternatives* (beyond the paper's own regression) are unresearched.
  This is the highest-value L3 target on evidence-quality grounds.
- **L3 — sit-bone width vs saddle width entirely absent**, despite being the measurement most
  directly tied to SDL-13's "get a wider saddle" recommendation. Both a literature and maker
  measurement systems exist.
- **L2/L3 — cleat and foot position is missing and is arguably misplaced by its absence.** Two
  sources in this round (myvelofit's ordering article, and the general fit-order consensus) state
  the fit should *start* at the feet, before saddle position. The corpus currently has no foot
  chapter at all. **Recommend to the coordinator: a `foot-cleat-fit.md` chapter, or an explicit
  scope decision that flat-pedal MTB makes it low priority** — note that flat pedals genuinely do
  reduce its urgency for this project's primary discipline, which may be the right answer.
- **L4 — no diagnosis craft.** Nothing yet on reading a rider's pedal stroke, spotting hip rock as
  a too-high indicator, or the iteration loop. Peloton Physio's "how do I know my saddle is
  optimal" checklist (stable pelvis, no side-to-side rock, no dead spot at the bottom, not
  constantly shifting position) is a fetched L4 seed that was **not** written up as facts this
  round and is the obvious first L4 target.
