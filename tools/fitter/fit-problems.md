# Fit Problems — Fitter Corpus

**Maturity: foundation** (L1 core complete and, unusually, the corpus's best-evidenced chapter —
every symptom family here carries peer-reviewed backing. What is thin is the *fit-cause* half:
the clinical literature establishes the conditions well and the specific positional corrections
much less well — see Gaps). Read [`INDEX.md`](INDEX.md) first (corpus rules, tiers,
⚠ GUIDANCE-NOT-PRESCRIPTION, the ID-range rule).

Fact IDs are stable and append-only (`PRB-n`).

---

> # ⚠ READ BEFORE USING THIS CHAPTER
>
> **Every entry below is a documented ASSOCIATION between a symptom and a candidate fit cause. It
> is a hypothesis to test by adjustment — never a diagnosis.**
>
> **Per INDEX rule 4, every answer drawn from this chapter MUST carry a professional-referral
> line.** Symptoms in the following classes are medical, not mechanical, until a clinician says
> otherwise: any **neurological** symptom (numbness, tingling, weakness, loss of sensation), any
> **persistent or worsening** pain, any **genital, perineal or sexual** symptom, and any symptom
> that **does not resolve when the fit change is reversed**. For these, the correct output is:
> here is the documented fit association, AND see a clinician and/or a professional bike fitter.
> That line is not boilerplate to be trimmed for brevity — it is the reason this chapter is
> allowed to exist.
>
> A second constraint follows from the evidence itself: **a symptom rarely has one cause.** The
> literature repeatedly attributes cycling overuse injury to a *combination* of inadequate
> preparation, inappropriate equipment, poor technique, and training volume. Fit is one of four.
> Presenting a fit cause as *the* cause is a misrepresentation of the sources.

## The general picture

**PRB-1 — Cycling overuse injury is common, fit is one of several contributing causes, and the
literature's own emphasis is on PREVENTION rather than correction.** A review of cycling lower-
extremity injuries lists knee pain, patellar/quadriceps tendinitis, iliotibial band syndrome, hip
pain, medial tibial stress syndrome, stress fracture, compartment syndrome, foot numbness and
metatarsalgia as common and **largely preventable**, and attributes them to a combination of
**inadequate preparation, inappropriate equipment, poor technique, and overuse** — with prevention
focused on bicycle fit and alignment, appropriate equipment, rider position and pedalling
mechanics, and appropriate training. A second review, on the cycling segment of triathlon, reports
cycling-related injuries account for **20% of all triathlon injuries**, that the majority of
cycling complaints are overuse or poor technique, that the knee, lower back, neck and Achilles
tendon are the most frequently affected sites, and states directly that **improper bike fit
contributes to causing a significant number of these conditions** and that bike geometry may be
altered to alleviate symptoms.
- ⚠ Note both are **review** articles about cycling generally, not MTB specifically, and neither
  is a controlled trial of fit interventions. They establish the *association* and the *clinical
  consensus*, not a dose-response.
- *Tier:* A. Sources: Wanich, Hodgkins, Columbier, Muraski & Kennedy, *J Am Acad Orthop Surg*
  2007;15(12):748–56 ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.5435/00124635-200712000-00008)); Deakon, *Sports Med
  Arthrosc Rev* 2012;20(4):200–5 ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1097/JSA.0b013e3182688fa0)). Retrieved
  via PubMed.

## Knee pain

**PRB-2 — Knee pain is the most-studied fit-associated symptom, and saddle height is the
most-studied fit cause — but the evidence connecting them is weaker than the popular account
suggests.** Established: overuse knee pain occurs in roughly **50% of cyclists**; anterior knee
pain is among the most common cycling diagnoses; **patellofemoral compressive force appears
inversely related to saddle height** (a *lower* saddle raises it); and a **5% saddle-height change
alters knee kinematics by ~35% and moments by ~16%**, while changes **under 4% of leg length**
produce trivial-to-small load changes. What is **not** established: both Bini reviews state
explicitly that information on saddle height's effect on lower-limb **injury risk** is *limited* —
the kinematic and force effects are measured, the injury outcomes largely are not.
- **The recommendation the literature does make**, conservatively: set saddle height by the
  **knee-flexion-angle method** to reduce knee-injury risk (the 2011 review gives 25–30°;
  ⚠ note per SDL-3 this is a **static-protocol** number and must not be applied to a dynamic
  measurement without the ~8° correction, and per SDL-1 the 2021 review favours dynamic
  measurement).
- **Direction-aware candidates to test**, from the fit sources: a saddle **too high** produces hip
  rock, lower-back ache and reaching at the bottom of the stroke (irritating knees and hips,
  especially when the bike moves under the rider on rough ground); a saddle **too low** loads the
  knee on every stroke and raises patellofemoral force. Saddle **fore/aft** also loads the knee —
  too far forward or back can feel fine early and hurt when tired and climbing — and an
  excessively **nose-down** saddle increases quad tension (and hence knee load) as the rider braces
  against sliding forward (SDL-13).
- ⚠ **Referral line required.** Persistent knee pain warrants a clinician; this is an association
  set to test, not a diagnosis.
- *Tier:* A (prevalence, force/kinematics, the limits of the evidence, the conservative
  recommendation) + B (the direction-aware candidates). Sources: Bini, Hume & Croft, *Sports Med*
  2011;41(6):463–76 ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.2165/11588740-000000000-00000)); Bini &
  Priego-Quesada, *J Sports Sci* 2021;40(4):386–400
  ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/02640414.2021.1994727)); Wanich et al. 2007 and Deakon 2012 (as
  PRB-1); Roadman Cycling (roadmancycling.com/blog/mtb-bike-fit-basics); Grava
  (gograva.com/blogs/guides/gravel-bike-fit-and-geometry); Peloton Physio
  (pelotonphysio.com.au/blog/saddle-position).

## Hand and wrist — cyclist's palsy

**PRB-3 — Hand numbness and weakness in cyclists has a specific, measured neurological basis, and
handlebar fit is part of its documented management.** *Cyclist's palsy* is a compression
neuropathy — **more commonly ulnar than median** — attributed to prolonged grip pressure on the
handlebars, classically an entrapment of the ulnar nerve at Guyon's canal. It has been measured
objectively: an electrophysiologic study of 28 hands before and after a 6-day, 420-mile tour found
**significantly prolonged distal motor latencies of the deep branch of the ulnar nerve** to the
first dorsal interosseous, while median motor/sensory and ulnar sensory studies did not change
significantly; three hands showed electrophysiologic and symptomatic **worsening of carpal tunnel
syndrome**, with one new onset. The clinical review's stated management is threefold and puts fit
first: **attending to bicycle fit, energy-absorbing gloves and handlebar treatments, and cycling
technique** — and it reports that the condition **seldom produces permanent injury or deficit if
promptly recognised and managed**. The same review notes overuse symptoms in wrist tendons and in
the connective tissues of neck and shoulder when bike design and fit couple with repetitive motion.
- **Fit causes to test**, from the fit sources: too much weight on the hands, which is produced by
  bars **too low** (COC-8), a cockpit **too long**, an excessively **nose-down saddle** (SDL-13),
  or a road position copied onto gravel (DSC-8); wrist **angle**, set by bar backsweep and roll —
  more backsweep (9–12° vs a typical 7–9°) reportedly resolves wrist pain or numbness outright for
  some riders (COC-10); and **grip diameter/material**, though no selection method is sourced
  (COC-13).
- ⚠ **Referral line required — emphatically.** Numbness, tingling and weakness are neurological
  symptoms. "Prompt recognition" is doing the work in that favourable prognosis. See a clinician.
- *Tier:* A (the neuropathy, the measurement, the management framing) + B (the specific fit
  causes). Sources: Richmond, *Clin Sports Med* 1994;13(1):165–73 (PMID 8111850); Akuthota,
  Plastaras, Lindberg, Tobey, Press & Garvan, *Am J Sports Med* 2005;33(8):1224–30
  ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/0363546505275131)). Retrieved via PubMed. Fit causes: Roadman
  Cycling, myvelofit *Saddle Tilt 101* (myvelofit.com/fit-academy/saddle-tilt-101/), Peloton
  Physio, Grava (as PRB-2), John Higgins / FitKit Systems
  (fitkitsystems.com/how-to-fit-a-mountain-bike-practical-techniques-and-essential-adjustments/).

## Perineal and genital symptoms

**PRB-4 — Perineal numbness and cycling-related sexual dysfunction are documented in both sexes,
with reported prevalences high enough that the topic cannot be treated as marginal — and the
evidence points at SADDLE and POSITION rather than at cycling itself.** A systematic review reports
**perineal numbness at 22–91% prevalence** and erectile dysfunction at **1.8–50%** among reported
male populations, with the primary mechanism in both sexes appearing to be **compression of the
pudendal nerve within Alcock's canal** (plus possible trauma). Its central finding for fit
purposes, stated as the best available evidence: **the type of saddle used and the rider's
position have more effect on resultant sexual dysfunction than participation in cycling itself.**
The review also calls for further validated studies on both description and prevention.
- **Fit causes to test**, from the fit sources: a **nose-up** saddle, which blocks forward pelvic
  rotation and increases pressure at the front (described by a fitting physio as almost never
  correct); a saddle **too high**, which can increase perineal pressure; and — the trap — an
  **excessively nose-down** saddle, which riders commonly adopt *to escape* perineal pressure and
  which **makes it worse** by sliding them onto the narrow part of the saddle with less pelvic
  support. The indicated corrections in the fit sources are a **different saddle** (often wider, or
  with a cutout) and/or **lowering** the saddle — not more down-tilt. Small tilt changes (~1°) are
  the stated unit of adjustment (SDL-12/SDL-13).
- ⚠ **Referral line required — mandatory and non-negotiable.** These are genital and neurological
  symptoms. Persistent perineal numbness or any sexual dysfunction warrants a clinician, and the
  saddle change is not a substitute for that consultation. The corpus states the association and
  stops.
- *Tier:* A (prevalence, mechanism, the saddle/position finding) + B (the specific tilt and saddle
  corrections). Sources: Baran, Mitchell & Hellstrom, *Sex Med Rev* 2015;2(3–4):93–101
  ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1002/smrj.32)). Retrieved via PubMed. Fit causes: Peloton Physio and
  myvelofit *Saddle Tilt 101* (as PRB-3).

**PRB-5 — MTB riders report fewer saddle-pressure problems than road riders, for a structural
reason.** Far fewer mountain bikers suffer saddle-pressure issues than road cyclists, simply
because they spend less time seated — though discomfort still occurs, particularly when the saddle
is a poor match for the rider. This does not make PRB-4 inapplicable to MTB; it changes the base
rate, and it is relevant to long seated XC and gravel riding rather than to technical trail riding
(DSC-10's split).
- *Tier:* B. Source: John Higgins / FitKit Systems (as PRB-3).

## Neck, back and shoulder

**PRB-6 — Lower back and neck are among the most frequently affected sites, and the fit causes
converge on bar height and cockpit length.** The clinical picture: lower back and neck myofascial
pain rank among the most common cycling diagnoses, alongside anterior knee pain, iliotibial band
friction syndrome and Achilles tendonitis. **Fit causes to test:** bars **too low**, producing
lower-back tension, fatigue and ache that shows up on long grinding climbs (COC-8); a cockpit
**too long**, or a frame the rider is cramped in — a fitter reports **torso-dominant riders** on
modern MTBs presenting with hand pressure, shoulder tension, backache and difficulty breathing
under exertion, caused by the frame's limited adjustment range (DSC-4/COC-9); a **nose-up saddle**,
which drives posterior pelvic rotation, increased spinal flexion, possible lower-back pain, and
over-reaching for the bars with consequent neck and shoulder pain (SDL-13); and, on gravel, a road
position copied wholesale (DSC-8).
- ⚠ Referral line required for persistent or radiating back or neck pain.
- *Tier:* A (the injury-site epidemiology) + B (the fit causes). Sources: Deakon 2012 (as PRB-1);
  John Higgins / FitKit Systems, Peloton Physio, Roadman Cycling (as PRB-3).

**PRB-7 — Shoulder symptoms have a bar-width association specifically, with a stated mechanism.**
A bar that is too wide drives the rider into elbows-up, shoulders-shrugged posture — the opposite
of the strong position (humerus externally rotated, scapula seated back and down) — which is poorer
for muscle activation and raises shoulder-joint injury risk; clinicians report seeing
**impingement-like symptoms (rotator-cuff irritation) and anterior shoulder pain (bicep
irritation)** in riders holding it. The coach who popularised the bar-width formula reports his own
shoulder problems worsening with the industry shift to wider bars, alongside other causes.
- ⚠ **Tier honesty:** this is the weakest-evidenced entry in the chapter — a clinician's published
  mechanism and clinical observation plus a coach's personal account, with **no Tier-A literature
  on bar width and shoulder pathology located** (see `cockpit-fit.md` Gaps). Present it as a
  plausible, mechanistically-argued association, not as established.
- ⚠ Referral line required for persistent shoulder pain.
- *Tier:* B. Sources: REI Expert Advice *Are Your Mountain Bike Handlebars Too Wide?*
  (rei.com/learn/expert-advice/are-your-handlebars-too-wide.html); Lee McCormack via Pinkbike
  (pinkbike.com/news/finding-your-sweet-spot-handlebar-width.html).

## Foot

**PRB-8 — Foot numbness and metatarsalgia are documented cycling overuse conditions, and this
corpus currently cannot say much about their fit causes.** Both appear in the clinical review's
list of common, largely preventable lower-extremity cycling conditions, with prevention pointing at
bicycle fit and alignment, appropriate equipment, and pedalling mechanics; non-surgical management
options listed include orthotics among others.
- ⚠ **Honest limit:** the dominant fit levers for foot symptoms are **cleat position, shoe fit and
  footbeds**, and this corpus has **no foot/cleat chapter** (flagged in `saddle-position.md` Gaps
  as a possible `foot-cleat-fit.md`). The corpus cannot currently give a grounded fit answer for
  foot numbness beyond naming the general prevention categories. Say so rather than improvise.
- ⚠ Referral line required — numbness is neurological.
- *Tier:* A (the conditions and prevention framing). Source: Wanich et al. 2007 (as PRB-1).

## How to use a fit-cause hypothesis (the method, not the answer)

**PRB-9 — The documented adjustment method is: change ONE thing, ride, reassess — and change it by
a small amount.** The consensus procedure across fit sources: work in a fixed order (saddle
tilt → height → fore/aft, having first set the feet, then the cockpit), make **one** change at a
time so you can attribute the result, keep changes small (1° of tilt; 5 mm per side on bar width),
photograph or note the starting point so any change is reversible, and re-check dimensions a change
is coupled to — notably re-checking saddle **height** after any significant **fore/aft** move,
since moving aft effectively raises the saddle and moving fore effectively lowers it. Fit is
explicitly described as iterative: you will not get everything right on the first attempt.
- ⚠ **The corollary that belongs in every answer:** if a fit change does not help, **reverse it**
  before trying the next one, and if nothing helps, that is itself informative — it is evidence the
  problem may not be a fit problem, which is the point at which the professional referral stops
  being a disclaimer and becomes the actual recommendation.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Sources: myvelofit *Bike Fit Adjustments: The right order*
  (myvelofit.com/fit-academy/bike-fitting-order-of-adjustments/) and *Saddle Tilt 101* (as PRB-3);
  BikeRadar (bikeradar.com/advice/sizing-and-fit/saddle-setback); Roadman Cycling (as PRB-3);
  Liv Cycling (liv-cycling.com/global/campaigns/how-to-cut-mtb-handlebars/23137) and PNW Components
  (pnwcomponents.com/blogs/news/how-to-find-proper-handlebar-width) for the 5 mm bar increments.

## Gaps

Honest list of what is missing to reach `professional` (L2) and beyond:

- **The asymmetry that defines this chapter: the CONDITIONS are Tier A, the FIT CORRECTIONS are
  mostly Tier B.** The clinical literature is good at establishing that cyclists get these
  conditions and at describing mechanism; it is much thinner on *which positional change fixes
  which symptom*. Every entry above reflects that split and says so. **A literature search
  specifically for fit-intervention outcome studies was not run this round** and is the single
  highest-value next action for this chapter — including the possibility that the honest result is
  a documented null.
- **Iliotibial band syndrome, Achilles tendonitis, hip pain, medial tibial stress syndrome and
  compartment syndrome are named in PRB-1 and never developed.** ITBS in particular has a
  commonly-cited saddle-height and cleat association that is not sourced here.
- **No foot/cleat coverage (PRB-8)** — the corpus's most conspicuous structural hole, since cleat
  position is the adjustment several sources say to set *first*.
- **No MTB-specific injury epidemiology.** Every source here is road/triathlon/general cycling. MTB
  has a substantially different injury profile (traumatic weighted far more heavily against
  overuse) and none of it is captured. Note this cuts both ways: it means the overuse emphasis here
  may *overstate* fit's share of MTB injury.
- **No female-specific symptom data**, despite SDL-5 establishing that the fit formulas themselves
  fail women, and despite PRB-4's source covering both sexes while the numbers quoted are largely
  male. A real evidence gap, and a priority.
- **Nothing on training load as the confounder.** PRB-1 names inadequate preparation and overuse as
  co-causes with equipment; a rider whose real problem is a volume spike will not be helped by a
  saddle adjustment, and the corpus has no material on distinguishing the two. This is L4
  diagnosis craft and the chapter's largest craft gap.
- **L4 — no observation craft.** Nothing on what a fitter *looks for* on a moving rider (hip rock,
  ankling, dropped shoulder, asymmetry). Peloton Physio's optimal-saddle-position checklist, cited
  in `saddle-position.md` Gaps, is the fetched seed for this and remains unwritten.
