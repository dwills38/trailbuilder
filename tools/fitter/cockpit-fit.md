# Cockpit Fit — Fitter Corpus

**Maturity: foundation** (L1 core complete: bar width with shoulder/biomechanical grounding, the
cut-stiffness coupling, stem-length↔handling, bar rise/sweep/roll, lever reach and angle. Grip
diameter is thin and lever ergonomics have no Tier-A backing — see Gaps). Read
[`INDEX.md`](INDEX.md) first (corpus rules, tiers, ⚠ GUIDANCE-NOT-PRESCRIPTION, the ID-range rule).

Fact IDs are stable and append-only (`COC-n`) — deliberately **not** `CKP-`, which the mechanic
corpus owns for cockpit *hardware* compatibility. This chapter is cockpit *fit*: the mechanic
corpus answers "does this bar fit this stem," this one answers "does this bar fit this rider."

Every fact carries its **tier** and a **Couplings** line where the dimension moves something else.

## Handlebar width

**COC-1 — Bar width is a biomechanical variable, not just a handling preference: too wide
degrades shoulder mechanics measurably.** The mechanism, given by a physiotherapist writing for
REI: a rider is strongest and most stable with the **humerus in external rotation and the scapula
seated slightly back and down**. A bar that is too wide drives the opposite — elbows up, shoulders
shrugged — which is poorer for muscle activation and raises injury risk at the shoulder joint;
clinicians report seeing impingement-like symptoms (rotator-cuff irritation) and anterior shoulder
pain in riders holding that position. The self-check offered: have someone film you descending
and look at whether your elbows are flared upward with shoulders shrugged.
- ⚠ **Tier note:** this is a clinician's published mechanism and clinical observation, not a
  controlled study. **No Tier-A literature on MTB bar width was located this round** — recorded in
  Gaps as the chapter's main evidence weakness.
- *Couplings:* bar width ↔ shoulder position ↔ ability to absorb front-wheel impacts (COC-2).
- *Tier:* B. Source: REI Expert Advice *Are Your Mountain Bike Handlebars Too Wide?*
  (rei.com/learn/expert-advice/are-your-handlebars-too-wide.html).

**COC-2 — Both too-wide AND too-narrow degrade the rider's ability to absorb impacts; the error is
direction-aware but not one-sided.** Running a bar too wide reduces the arms' capacity to absorb
front-wheel impacts (the rider is effectively doing push-ups while riding, and a too-wide stance
is a weak one); running it too narrow does exactly the same. A coach's list of what a wrong width
costs: lost range of motion (so steep descents and tight turns get harder), lost pushing strength
for handling impacts, lost pulling strength for pumping terrain, reliance on the small muscles of
the arms and shoulders instead of the torso — hence earlier fatigue — and potential shoulder
damage.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Sources: off-road.cc / road.cc *How to cut your mountain bike handlebar*
  (road.cc/offroad/feature/how-to-cut-your-mountain-bike-handlebar-carbon-and-aluminium-mtb-bar-
  trimming-explained); Lee McCormack via Pinkbike
  (pinkbike.com/news/finding-your-sweet-spot-handlebar-width.html).

**COC-3 — The published starting points for width, recorded as competing methods.** Per
⚠ GUIDANCE-NOT-PRESCRIPTION these are listed with their derivations, not merged.
- **Race Face (maker):** consider height and shoulder width; taller/broader riders tend toward
  ~800 mm, shorter or narrower-shouldered riders toward 760–780 mm or narrower. Discipline shifts
  it — gravity riders prefer higher/wider, XC lower and narrower, trail in between.
- **PNW Components (maker):** no one-size-fits-all equation; taller, heavier, broader-shouldered
  riders generally need wider. The target is a position where wrists, elbows and shoulders align
  and the rider keeps control of the front wheel.
- **Liv (maker, women's-specific):** a good width lets the elbows keep a relaxed bend, "usually a
  bit wider than your shoulders"; for most female riders they place that at **700–760 mm**.
- **The push-up test (practitioner):** get into a proper-form push-up position and measure between
  the hands — that hand spacing is the starting point.
- **RideLogic "sweet spot" formula (⚠ Tier B-vendor):** height in mm × **0.440** (male) or
  × **0.426** (female). Its author states this is a **maximum** recommended width, that the usable
  range runs from the sweet spot to about **5% narrower**, and — importantly — that going *wider*
  than it is "almost all disadvantages." Labelled vendor because RideLogic is a commercial fit
  system, and note it is a **single-variable height formula**, which SDL-6's Tier-A evidence
  treats as a weak predictor class.
- **Observed practice, for calibration only (Tier D):** component-brand staff and coaches
  independently report most trail riders landing **760–780 mm**, with 800 mm common as a stock
  width rather than a chosen one.
- *Tier:* A-mfr for Race Face / PNW / Liv; B for the push-up test; **B-vendor** for RideLogic;
  D for the observed-practice band. Sources: Race Face *Cockpit Selection and Setup Guide*
  (raceface.com/blogs/support/cockpit-selection-and-setup-guide); PNW Components *How to Find
  Proper Handlebar Width* (pnwcomponents.com/blogs/news/how-to-find-proper-handlebar-width); Liv
  *How to Cut MTB Handlebars* (liv-cycling.com/global/campaigns/how-to-cut-mtb-handlebars/23137);
  off-road.cc (as COC-2); Pinkbike/McCormack (as COC-2).

**COC-4 — Bar width and stem length are ONE decision, because changing width changes the
steering.** A narrower bar wakes the front end up — twitchier, easier to manoeuvre; a wider bar
calms it and adds straight-line stability. The maker-published consequence: if you want to keep
the handling you had, **counteract a narrower bar with a longer stem, and a wider bar with a
shorter stem**. A second stated caveat: a bike's published reach assumes the manufacturer's
intended stem and bar geometry, so changing bar width changes how the bike rides relative to its
own spec.
- *Couplings:* this is the chapter's central coupling and it reaches back into sizing — SIZ-8's
  "longer reach + shorter stem beats shorter reach + longer stem" is the same trade seen from the
  frame side.
- *Tier:* A-mfr. Source: PNW Components (as COC-3).

**COC-5 — Cutting a bar makes it stiffer, by a measured amount, and that is a fit consequence not
just a durability one.** Lab testing by MERIDA across bars from multiple brands found stiffness
increases by **about 10% for every 20 mm** removed — for both aluminium and carbon. Trimming an
800 mm bar to 740 mm therefore yields roughly a **30% stiffness increase**, materially changing
compliance. The recommendation that follows: **buy a bar close to your intended final width**
rather than cutting a wide one down, since makers may design different widths for appropriate flex
rather than selling a cut-down 800 mm bar; the source suggests treating ~20 mm as the sensible
limit for trimming.
- ⚠ **Direction-aware and irreversible:** cutting is one-way. Every source agrees on the
  procedure — start wide, remove **5 mm per side at a time**, ride, reassess.
- *Couplings:* width ↔ stiffness ↔ hand/wrist comfort. A rider who cuts to fix shoulder position
  (COC-1) may create a harshness problem they then misattribute to grips or tyre pressure.
- *Tier:* B (reporting maker lab data). Source: ENDURO Mountainbike Magazine *In the handlebar
  jungle* (enduro-mtb.com/en/mtb-handlebar-guide/).

**COC-6 — Makers publish minimum widths and cut guides; those are hard limits, not suggestions.**
OneUp's carbon bars carry a stated **minimum width of 740 mm** with 5 mm guide marks from
740–800 mm on both ends. Liv publishes the tooling requirement: a pipe cutter for aluminium; for
carbon a **hacksaw with a tungsten-carbide blade** with masking tape over the cut mark to stop
fraying, a lock-on grip as a straight-cut guide, and filing of sharp edges. A safety note appears
across sources: testing a narrower position by sliding grips inward leaves the bar ends exposed
and **must not be ridden that way** — cut and fit end plugs before real riding.
- *Tier:* A-mfr. Sources: OneUp Components *Cutting OneUp Carbon Handlebars Instructions*
  (oneupcomponents.com/blogs/cockpit-service-install/cutting-oneup-carbon-handlebars-instructions);
  Liv (as COC-3); PNW Components (as COC-3) for the exposed-bar-end warning.

## Bar height, rise, sweep and roll

**COC-7 — On a mountain bike the bar is usually ABOVE the saddle, and the useful measure is
saddle-to-grip rise rather than the road bike's saddle-to-bar drop.** A fitter reports expecting
grip height relative to saddle height to fall between **4 cm below and 8 cm above** saddle height,
depending on rider, bike and style. Bar rise itself spans **0–80 mm** across available bars —
making it the single largest height adjustment available, and the most expensive if it means a
new bar. Typical: XC bikes ship flat bars (0–10 mm rise) to encourage an aggressive position;
trail bikes commonly 20–30 mm.
- *Couplings:* two documented outliers show why the number is proportional, not absolute — riders
  with **long legs relative to height** run a high saddle and often need more rise to match, while
  **short riders on XS/S frames with long-travel forks** (which raise frame stack) can end up with
  ape-hanger bars and need *less* rise.
- *Tier:* B. Source: John Higgins / FitKit Systems *How to Fit a Mountain Bike*
  (fitkitsystems.com/how-to-fit-a-mountain-bike-practical-techniques-and-essential-adjustments/).

**COC-8 — Too-low bars have a specific, direction-aware failure signature.** With the bar too low:
too much weight sits on the hands, making it hard to lighten or loft the front wheel over
obstacles; lower-back tension, fatigue and ache show up on long grinding climbs; and on steep
descents the rider's weight is pulled too far forward, so staying centred requires overextending
arms and spine, which compromises both responsiveness and handling. A higher bar moves weight back
to counter steep descent angles.
- ⚠ Note the asymmetry: the *too-high* failure mode is not documented with equal specificity in
  the sources fetched — recorded in Gaps. Do not invent a symmetric answer (INDEX rule 8).
- *Tier:* B. Source: John Higgins / FitKit Systems (as COC-7).

**COC-9 — Modern MTB reach adjustment range is roughly HALF what a drop-bar bike offers, which
constrains what a fitter can fix.** Longer top tubes plus shorter stems mean flat-bar MTBs have at
most ~30 mm of effective reach adjustment against a drop-bar bike's ~60 mm. Consequence: the bike
design dictates the rider's position more than on other bike types, and the riders hurt worst are
**torso-dominant riders**, who end up cramped or front-heavy (steep seat angles pushing them
forward over the BB). Reported symptoms of that squeeze: increased hand pressure, shoulder
tension, backache, and difficulty breathing under exertion. The fitter's stated remedy is
pragmatic and against convention — fitting a **60 or 70 mm stem** on a bike that shipped with 40
or 50 mm, accepting that designers would object.
- ⚠ **This directly tensions with SIZ-8/COC-4's "shorter stem is better" consensus.** Both are
  Tier B. The reconciliation the sources support: short stems are right for *handling* on a
  correctly-sized frame; a longer stem is a *remedy* when the frame cannot be made to fit the
  rider's torso. Not tagged DISPUTED because they answer different questions — but state both.
- *Couplings:* bar height ↔ reach: a lower bar tends to go with a longer reach, a higher bar with
  a shorter one, and both change torso angle and weight distribution.
- *Tier:* B. Source: John Higgins / FitKit Systems (as COC-7).

**COC-10 — Bar rise, backsweep and roll all change wrist angle, and backsweep is an under-used
fix for wrist pain.** Rise sets hand height; **backsweep** is how far the bar sweeps back toward
the rider at the grips and directly sets wrist angle. Most trail bars run **20–30 mm rise and
7–9° backsweep**; riders with wrist pain or numbness sometimes resolve it entirely by moving to
**more backsweep (9–12°)**. **Bar roll** — rotation in the stem clamp — has a disproportionate
effect for how small the adjustment is; the stated starting point is to level the flat section
behind the rise, then adjust by feel, with rolling slightly forward opening the wrist angle and
feeling more natural on steep descents.
- *Couplings:* roll also moves the levers, so lever angle (COC-12) must be re-set after any roll
  change — the two are one adjustment in practice.
- *Tier:* B. Source: Roadman Cycling *MTB Bike Fit Basics*
  (roadmancycling.com/blog/mtb-bike-fit-basics).

## Stem length

**COC-11 — Stem length trades steering speed against front-wheel weighting, and modern MTBs are
designed around short stems.** Shorter stems make steering faster and more responsive and give
better front-wheel control on steep terrain; longer stems slow and stabilise steering but can put
too much weight over the front wheel on descents. Trail and enduro bikes are typically designed
around **35–50 mm**. The diagnostic offered: in a natural standing attack position, the arms
should be slightly bent with weight balanced between hands and feet — stretched out and
straight-armed means the cockpit is too long; cramped and hunched means too short. A rider needing
a stem longer than ~60 mm may have a frame whose reach is too short (see COC-9 for the
countervailing fitter view).
- *Tier:* B. Source: Roadman Cycling (as COC-10).

## Brake levers and grips

**COC-12 — Lever angle and reach are fit adjustments with stated targets, and they are checked in
the STANDING position, not seated.** *Angle:* set so that standing in the attack position with
arms slightly bent, the forearm and the braking finger(s) form a natural straight line — most
riders end up **slightly downward from horizontal**; too flat bends the wrist uncomfortably, too
steep loses leverage. *Reach:* most hydraulic levers have a reach-adjust screw — set it so the
lever can be comfortably reached and pulled to engagement **with one finger** while keeping a
secure grip, without the lever contacting the other fingers.
- ⚠ **Cross-corpus link:** lever *position* is fit (here); lever *compatibility* with a shifter
  clamp is mechanical (mechanic corpus, and engine rule 19 I-Spec/MatchMaker). Do not conflate.
- *Couplings:* lever position must be re-set after any bar roll (COC-10) and after any width
  change that moves the controls inboard (COC-6).
- *Tier:* B. Source: Roadman Cycling (as COC-10).

**COC-13 — Grip diameter and hand pressure are documented as a hand-symptom variable, but the fit
guidance located this round is thin.** The clinical literature establishes the mechanism —
prolonged grip pressure on handlebars is implicated in cyclist's palsy (see `fit-problems.md`
PRB-3) — and management is described as attending to **bicycle fit, energy-absorbing gloves and
handlebar treatments, and cycling technique**. Grips are also noted as one of the components whose
deviation from the designer's intended size affects the rider's centre of gravity, handling and
safety. **No source giving a diameter-selection method (e.g. hand-size → grip OD) was located this
round** — see Gaps.
- ⚠ Any hand numbness or tingling carries the professional-referral line; see PRB-3.
- *Tier:* A (mechanism/management, from the clinical review) + B (component-deviation caveat).
  Sources: Richmond, *Clin Sports Med* 1994;13(1):165–73 (PMID 8111850), retrieved via PubMed;
  Seven Cycles (sevencycles.com/articles/frame-reach-or-top-tube-length.php) for the
  component-deviation caveat.

## Gaps

Honest list of what is missing to reach `professional` (L2) and beyond:

- **No Tier-A evidence for ANY claim in the bar-width section.** COC-1's shoulder mechanism is a
  clinician's published reasoning; COC-3's formulas are maker and vendor assertions; COC-5's
  stiffness figure is one manufacturer's lab test reported second-hand. This is the chapter's
  defining weakness and must be stated in any bar-width answer. A literature search specifically
  for handlebar width vs shoulder EMG / impingement was **not** run this round and is the single
  highest-value next action for this chapter. (REI's article mentions clinicians using wireless
  EMG to assess activation across bar sizes — suggesting such literature may exist.)
- **COC-8 asymmetry — the too-high bar failure mode is undocumented here.** COC-7's outlier note
  (short riders with ape-hangers) hints at it but no source described the symptom set. Do not fill
  this by symmetry; fetch it.
- **COC-13 grip diameter has no selection method.** Needs a hand-anthropometry or ergonomics
  source; grip *material/pattern* vs vibration is likewise absent.
- **L2 — no per-discipline cockpit numbers beyond Race Face's one-line gravity/trail/XC sketch.**
  DH, dirt jump and BMX cockpits differ substantially and are unwritten. **[DJ]/[BMX] tagging will
  be needed** when they are added, per the sibling corpora's convention.
- **L2 — nothing on how suspension sag changes bar height under load**, the same MTB-specific gap
  flagged in `sizing-fundamentals.md`. A fork sitting 20–30% into travel lowers the bar relative
  to the static geometry every number in COC-7 is quoted against.
- **L3 — no anthropometry linking shoulder/biacromial width to bar width empirically.** Every
  method in COC-3 either uses *height* as a proxy or says "measure your shoulders" without giving
  the landmark or the offset. Biacromial-breadth anthropometric data exists and has not been
  brought in — this would convert COC-3 from assertion to derivation.
- **L4 — no diagnosis craft.** Reading a rider's cockpit from video (REI's elbow/shoulder check in
  COC-1 is the one seed present), and the order in which cockpit adjustments should be made, are
  both unwritten.
