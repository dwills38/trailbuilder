## The fault line

**DSC-1 — The organizing difference between road/XC fit and technical-MTB fit is SEATED-STATIC vs
STANDING-DYNAMIC, and it changes which measurements even matter.** On the road, the bike is set up
for sustained power output in a relatively static seated position; on technical trail, the rider is
constantly moving — standing, sitting, shifting fore and aft, absorbing impacts, cornering — and
the fit has to permit all of that while keeping them balanced. Stated at its sharpest by a fitter:
for cross-country riding on smooth non-technical trail the position can resemble a road/gravel/
cyclocross position (with a flat bar); that same position "will not do you much good" for ripping
down bumpy trails, riding a pump track, or climbing technical features.
- **The measurement consequence.** Seated fit is described by four parameters — saddle height,
  saddle setback, saddle-to-bar reach, saddle-to-bar drop. For technical riding the rider is not on
  the saddle at all when it matters, so the meaningful geometry is the **feet-to-hands**
  relationship (see SIZ-9's RAD framing, ⚠ Tier B-vendor), and saddle-to-bar reach/drop become
  *incidental* outputs rather than targets.
- ⚠ **This is the reconciliation the corpus offers for the SIZ-5 reach-vs-ETT dispute:** ETT
  describes the seated cockpit, reach describes the standing one. Which leads is a *discipline*
  question, not a correctness question.
- *Couplings:* it is not one fit or the other — a trail bike needs **both**, set in order. The
  documented sequence in the RideLogic-based practice: dial the standing relationship first, then
  set seat height for seated pedalling, and accept the saddle-to-bar numbers that fall out.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Sources: Zinn Cycles *Dynamic Mountain Bike Fitting*
  (blog.zinn.bike/dynamic-mountain-bike-fitting); Roadman Cycling *MTB Bike Fit Basics*
  (roadmancycling.com/blog/mtb-bike-fit-basics).

## MTB vs road

**DSC-2 — MTB saddle height is set slightly LOWER than the same rider's road height, and there is
a stated mechanism.** Two independent practitioner sources give the same adjustment and reason.
The magnitude reported is **5–10 mm below** a road-derived inseam formula. The mechanisms:
(a) rough trails thrust the bike upward into the rider, producing micro-levitation off the saddle
that **effectively raises** saddle height — more pronounced on a hardtail than full suspension;
(b) the rider needs clearance to move on rough terrain and is out of the saddle often anyway.
Gravel is grouped with MTB on this point by one of the sources.
- ⚠ **Tier honesty, and it matters:** this is **Tier B only**. Every Tier-A saddle-height study in
  `saddle-position.md` measured road cyclists, indoor cyclists, or ergometers. **No peer-reviewed
  source validating an off-road saddle-height offset was located.** State that when answering.
  Note also the offset is small relative to SDL-7's finding that changes under ~4% of leg length
  produce trivial load and no efficiency effect — for an 80 cm inseam, 4% is ~32 mm, so a 5–10 mm
  MTB drop sits **inside** the band the literature says is not measurably consequential. That does
  not make it wrong — comfort and clearance are not the same outcome as load and VO₂ — but it does
  mean it should be offered as a comfort/clearance preference, not as a load-reduction claim.
- *Couplings:* the micro-levitation mechanism means the correct offset depends on **suspension** —
  more for a hardtail than full-suspension. That is the corpus's clearest instance of a *bike*
  property changing a *rider* number, and it is unquantified in the sources.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Sources: John Higgins / FitKit Systems *How to Fit a Mountain Bike*
  (fitkitsystems.com/how-to-fit-a-mountain-bike-practical-techniques-and-essential-adjustments/);
  Roadman Cycling (as DSC-1).

**DSC-3 — MTB is more fit-FORGIVING than road, and simultaneously HARDER to fit well.** Both halves
are documented. Forgiving: less time seated means fewer saddle-pressure problems than road
cyclists suffer, and a wider band of acceptable positions. Harder: bike and equipment design
constrain the fitter more (see DSC-4), the riding is dynamic rather than static, MTB geometry
varies more between makers and model years than road/gravel/tri geometry does, and many bike
fitters come from a road-racing or triathlon background and may not have experienced the
difference a good MTB fit makes. The practical result reported: mountain bikers rarely seek a fit
until something is clearly wrong — the bike feels "off," or a recurring pain seems bike-related.
- *Tier:* B. Source: John Higgins / FitKit Systems (as DSC-2).

**DSC-4 — MTB offers materially LESS adjustment range than drop-bar bikes, in two dimensions at
once.** *Reach:* modern MTB geometry (long top tubes, short stems) leaves at most ~30 mm of
effective reach adjustment against a drop-bar bike's ~60 mm (COC-9). *Setback:* MTB seatposts —
droppers especially — are typically **zero-offset**, and MTB saddles tend to have shorter rails,
so rearward saddle adjustment is limited in a way road and gravel (with 15 mm or adjustable
15–35 mm setback posts available) are not (SDL-11). Steepening MTB seat-tube angles make the
setback squeeze worse over time.
- ⚠ **The consequence is a fit doctrine, not a footnote:** because the adjustment range is narrow,
  **frame size selection carries more of the fit burden on a mountain bike than on a road bike.**
  Getting the frame wrong cannot be fully corrected with components — which is exactly what SIZ-9's
  RAD framing asserts from the other direction, and why SIZ-8's between-sizes guidance matters more
  here than it would on a road bike.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Sources: John Higgins / FitKit Systems (as DSC-2); BikeRadar
  *How to set saddle setback* (bikeradar.com/advice/sizing-and-fit/saddle-setback).

**DSC-5 — Saddle choice transfers between disciplines only partly, because pelvic angle changes.**
A road saddle can be used on a mountain bike and MTB-specific saddles exist; whether the *same*
saddle works on both is a "maybe" — the pelvic angle, and therefore the pressure distribution,
differs between an aggressive road position and an upright MTB one. The same mechanism is stated
independently for gravel: the more upright torso rotates the pelvis backward relative to an
aggressive road position, moving where and how the sit bones contact the saddle, so a saddle that
is perfect on the road bike can be uncomfortable on the gravel bike.
- *Couplings:* torso angle → pelvic rotation → saddle contact. This is also why SDL-13's tilt
  doctrine is not portable across disciplines.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Sources: John Higgins / FitKit Systems (as DSC-2); bicyclenest
  *Gravel Bike Fit Differences From Road Bikes Explained*
  (bicyclenest.com/gravel-bike-fit-differences-from-road-bikes-explained/).

## Gravel — between the two, and defined by duration

**DSC-6 — Gravel fit is shorter and higher than road fit, and the driving variable is DURATION on
variable surfaces rather than terrain difficulty.** Gravel geometry relaxes the road position via
coordinated changes: a taller head tube (reported ~15–30 mm more than an equivalent road frame),
reach shorter by ~10–20 mm, higher stack, slacker seat-tube angle, longer wheelbase. Expressed as
a ratio, a 56 cm road race bike at 565 stack / 390 reach gives ~1.45, while a same-size gravel bike
at ~595 / 380 gives ~1.57. The practitioner-side summary from a fitter running both: the gravel
position is often **shorter and higher**; saddle height is often the **same or slightly lower**,
and/or a bit further forward, depending on cranks, pedals and cleat position.
- ⚠ **Tier caution on the specific figures:** the geometry deltas above are from a secondary source
  citing averaged manufacturer charts, not from fetched charts themselves. Treat the *direction*
  as consensus and the *magnitudes* as indicative pending a primary sweep (Gaps).
- **Why, stated as mechanism:** gravel rides are long mixed-effort efforts on unpredictable
  surfaces, so fit must protect against **cumulative** stress — neck tension, hand numbness, lower
  back fatigue, knee irritation — and must stay predictable when the rider is tired. A position
  that feels fine fresh can be unsafe fatigued.
- *Couplings:* the "danger zone" framing ties this chapter to `fit-problems.md`: gravel fit errors
  present as the PRB symptoms, delayed, rather than as immediate discomfort.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Sources: bicyclenest (as DSC-5); Endurance Collective *Road bike fit vs
  gravel bike fit* (publication.endurancecollective.co/p/road-bike-fit-vs-gravel-bike-fit);
  Grava *Gravel Bike Fit & Geometry Explained* (gograva.com/blogs/guides/gravel-bike-fit-and-geometry).

**DSC-7 — Gravel bars flare, and flare is a fit dimension that road bars do not have.** Most
gravel-specific bars carry **16–24° of flare** in the drops, widening the hand position there by
roughly 4–8 cm relative to the hoods, for a more stable platform on technical descents and rough
ground; standard road bars keep width constant for aerodynamics. Gravel bar reach and drop also
trend shorter and shallower than road (a road bar at ~80 mm reach / 130 mm drop against a gravel
bar at ~70 / 115), making it easier to change hand position without disrupting balance. The
trade-off is stated: flared drops add frontal area and a less aggressive low position, which
matters if the bike also sees group road rides.
- *Couplings:* flare means a gravel bar has **two** effective widths (hoods and drops), so a single
  bar-width number does not describe it — unlike the flat bar in `cockpit-fit.md`.
- *Tier:* B. Source: bicyclenest (as DSC-5).

**DSC-8 — The most commonly reported gravel fit error is copying the road position wholesale.**
Named errors: bars too low and reach too long (dumping weight into the hands); saddle too high —
specifically problematic on rough terrain where the bike moves underneath the rider (the same
mechanism as DSC-2); "fast-looking" slammed-stem decisions that hold for an hour then fall apart;
and not accounting for fatigue. The stated countermeasures for a road→gravel transfer: increase
saddle-to-bar **stack by 20–40 mm** (conservative end for gravel racing, higher for adventure
riding) and reduce effective **reach by 10–25 mm** via a shorter stem.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Sources: Grava (as DSC-6); bicyclenest (as DSC-5).

## Within MTB — the sub-disciplines

**DSC-9 — Cockpit choice shifts by MTB sub-discipline in a documented direction, though the
numbers are preference-tier.** Race Face's own guidance: **gravity** riders tend to prefer **higher**
bars for confidence on steeps; **XC** riders prefer **low** bars for climbing efficiency; **trail**
sits between. On width, XC narrower (a component-brand staffer reports running 760 mm on XC bikes
and finding a wide bar "super weird" there, against 780 mm on trail) and gravity wider, with
narrower bars favoured for tight/wooded terrain regardless of discipline. Bar rise by category
follows: XC bikes commonly ship **flat bars (0–10 mm rise)** to encourage an aggressive position;
trail bikes commonly **20–30 mm**.
- ⚠ **Tier D on the specific widths** — these are practitioner and staff preferences, explicitly
  framed as such by their sources, not measured recommendations. Race Face's directional guidance
  is A-mfr; the numbers attached to it are not.
- *Tier:* A-mfr (direction) + D (numbers). Sources: Race Face *Cockpit Selection and Setup Guide*
  (raceface.com/blogs/support/cockpit-selection-and-setup-guide); PNW Components
  (pnwcomponents.com/blogs/news/how-to-find-proper-handlebar-width); John Higgins / FitKit Systems
  (as DSC-2) for the rise-by-category figures.

**DSC-10 — Within MTB, the same fault line as DSC-1 reappears between XC and everything else.** A
fitter draws the line not between bike categories but between **extended seated riding** (long
cross-country, dirt-road) and **dynamic riding** (trails with rocks, roots, rollers, corners,
berms, technical climbs, long descents, plus pump tracks and bike parks), and offers the two as
**separate fitting services**. The XC/dirt-road fit is essentially the road/gravel/cyclocross
four-parameter fit with a flat bar; the dynamic fit is the standing feet-to-hands fit.
- ⚠ **Practical consequence for any fit surface this project builds:** "mountain bike" is not one
  fit target. Asking *what kind of riding* is not a nicety — it selects which method applies. A
  tool that asked only for height and offered one MTB answer would be wrong for one of the two
  populations, and the corpus should say so rather than average them.
- *Tier:* B. Source: Zinn Cycles (as DSC-1).

## Gaps

Honest list of what is missing to reach `professional` (L2) and beyond:

- **Every fact in this chapter is Tier B or below except Race Face's directional cockpit
  guidance.** There is no peer-reviewed discipline-comparison work here at all. **No literature
  search for off-road vs road fit was run this round** — this is the chapter's defining weakness
  and the first thing an L2 round should attempt, even if the result is a documented null.
- **DSC-2's MTB offset is unquantified against suspension type.** Sources say hardtails want more
  drop than full-suspension; nobody says how much. Given the catalog already discriminates
  `suspension: 'full' | 'hardtail'`, this is a gap with an obvious product hook if it can be
  sourced.
- **DSC-6's gravel geometry deltas need a primary sweep.** Currently secondary-sourced averages;
  fetching maker geometry charts directly (Trek, Specialized, Canyon, Cannondale — all named as
  the averaged set) would promote them to A-mfr.
- **DH, dirt jump and BMX fit are entirely absent.** DJ and BMX fit is *qualitatively different* —
  the bike is deliberately undersized, the saddle is often not a contact point at all, and seated
  pedalling position is nearly irrelevant. Given BMX go-live (2026-07-17) and the DJ category
  already being live, this is a real product gap. **Facts must be tagged `[BMX]` / `[DJ]`** per the
  sibling corpora's convention when added.
- **Road fit itself is thin** — it appears here mainly as the thing MTB and gravel are contrasted
  *against*, never developed in its own right. Acceptable while MTB is the flagship priority, but
  it will not survive the road/gravel expansion.
- **L2 — no e-bike content, and none is wanted** (hard rule 1). Recorded so a future round does not
  read the absence as an oversight.
- **L3 — nothing on how discipline interacts with rider anthropometry.** Whether a long-femured
  rider is differentially disadvantaged on a steep-seat-angle enduro bike, for instance, is exactly
  the kind of question DSC-4's squeeze raises and this chapter cannot answer.
