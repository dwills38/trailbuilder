# Cornering — Coach Corpus

**Maturity: foundation** (L1 core solid; L2 corner-types thin). Flat · bermed · off-camber
corners · line choice; switchbacks cross-referenced to `terrain-features.md`. Read
[`INDEX.md`](INDEX.md) first (corpus rules, tiers, SAFETY-FIRST PROGRESSION, tacit-layer
disclaimer).

Fact IDs are stable and append-only (`COR-n`). Every skill fact carries **Prereq / Gear /
Progression**. Cornering is widely called *the* most impactful skill a rider can develop — where
most trail time is spent and most speed is made or lost — and the L1 technique is unusually
well-corroborated across independent coaches (this chapter's facts are Tier B-consensus).

## The four fundamentals (they assemble one corner)

**COR-1 — VISION: look through the corner to the EXIT — the bike goes where your eyes go
(mechanical, not motivational).** Turn head and eyes early to the exit before you turn in. The
chain is physical: eyes → upper body follows the eye line → weight follows the upper body → bike
tracks the weight. A rider looking at the front wheel is physically steering toward the inside;
a rider looking at the exit steers toward the exit. It feels unnatural at first — push through it.
- *Prereq:* FND-6 (vision base).
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Progression:* pick one familiar corner; for the first passes focus *only* on locking eyes to
  the exit before turn-in, nothing else (single-focus drill — see COR-6).
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: DavidMTB (*"Your bike goes where your eyes go"*), promountainbike,
  WildSide, thetoliver.com — all four teach eyes-to-exit as mechanical fact.

**COR-2 — BRAKE BEFORE, not during: arrive with hands off the brakes.** Set speed on the approach,
in a straight line, on grippy ground; release the brakes as you reach turn-in and carry momentum
through the arc. This is the grip budget (BRK-5) applied: grip spent braking is grip not available
to turn. When learning, enter feeling *slightly too slow* — the natural tendency is to over-carry
speed. Most slide-outs and wide exits trace back to braking too late on the approach.
- *Prereq:* BRK-4 (brake-before), BRK-5 (grip budget), FND-6.
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Progression:* on an easy corner, practise braking to target speed on the straight and entering
  with hands off the levers → only then add speed.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: WildSide (*most important part happens before the turn*; tires brake
  OR turn), promountainbike, thetoliver.com, DavidMTB (brake before, release at entry).

**COR-3 — OUTSIDE FOOT DOWN at 6 o'clock and WEIGHTED; inside foot up.** Drop the outside pedal to
straight-down and press weight through it. This does two things at once: lowers the centre of
gravity (stability) and drives weight down through the crank and bottom bracket *into the tyres*
from inside the bike — loading the contact patch (grip) more directly than weighting from above.
It also lifts the inside pedal clear of the ground so you can lean the bike further without a pedal
strike. Most effective in corners tighter than ~90° and on slick/off-camber sections.
- *Prereq:* FND-5 (heels/feet weighting), FND-7 (bike-body separation).
- *Gear:* helmet; flat pedals reward correct foot weighting while learning.
- *Progression:* first passes focus *only* on dropping and weighting the outside foot — nothing
  else (single-focus drill, COR-6).
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: DavidMTB (outside pedal to 6 o'clock, weighted — *"this single
  change will immediately feel different and better"*), promountainbike, WildSide, Bike Network
  (why/when/how to drop the outside pedal).

**COR-4 — LEAN THE BIKE MORE THAN THE BODY (bike-body separation) — not the road-bike full-lean.**
Road cyclists lean bike and body as one unit; MTB cornering is different — you angle the *bike* into
the corner while the body stays relatively upright, hips pressed toward the inside, outside elbow
slightly raised, driving the inside bar forward. This keeps your centre of gravity over the contact
patch (traction) rather than committing it to the lean. Steer by *leaning the bike*, not by turning
the bars sharply.
- *Prereq:* FND-7 (bike-body separation), COR-3.
- *Gear:* helmet; knee pads reasonable as lean/speed increase.
- *Progression:* introduce on mellow berms (which hold you) feeling the bike tip under a stable
  torso → carry to flat corners where the separation must create its own grip.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: DavidMTB (lean bike more than body, "bike-body separation"),
  thetoliver.com (angle the bike, body stable), promountainbike (hips press into the corner).
  **Note the walked-back cue below.**

**COR-5 — ⚠ Coaching update: DON'T flare the outside elbow wide anymore.** Older instruction said
to flare the elbow wide in corners; most skills coaches have walked this back because it *limits*
range of motion. Keep elbows relaxed and slightly bent, not deliberately winged out. Recorded as a
correction so the corpus teaches the current cue, not the retired one.
- *Prereq:* COR-4.
- *Tier:* B. Source: WildSide (*"most skills coaches have since walked that advice back"*). This is
  a coaching-evolution note, not a ⚠ DISPUTE (sources agree on the current cue).

## The corner as a sequence

**COR-6 — The six-step corner (assemble the fundamentals in order), taught one focus at a time.**
1. **Spot** the corner and its exit early (COR-1/FND-6).
2. **Brake** to target speed on the approach; release at entry (COR-2).
3. **Drop the outside pedal**, weight it (COR-3).
4. **Look through** — eyes to the exit, not the ground ahead (COR-1).
5. **Lean the bike** into the corner, body relatively upright (COR-4).
6. **Drive the exit** — as the exit opens, look further ahead and put the power down.

The **teaching method is one focus per pass**: e.g. 5 passes on outside-foot only, 5 on vision
only, 5 on brake-before only, then 5 combining — the canonical single-focus corner drill. And
**practise slow**: at very low speed you can *feel* where your weight is, whether the outside pedal
is down, and whether you're looking through. Slow, deliberate repetition on ONE corner builds
better habits than fast full laps; coaches walk, slow-roll, then build speed.
- *Prereq:* COR-1..4.
- *Gear:* helmet; low-speed practice keeps the risk low while the pattern is built.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: DavidMTB (the six steps + the 5×/5×/5×/5× single-focus drill),
  WildSide (slow deliberate repetition beats fast laps), promountainbike (one focus per run).

## Corner types (L1 seed; L2 depth is a gap)

**COR-7 — FLAT corners rely on your technique for grip; BERMS give you a wall to lean on.** In a
flat turn there's no bank to hold you, so outside-foot weighting and bike lean must generate the
grip — lean the bike aggressively while the body stays balanced. A berm (banked corner) provides a
supporting wall: you can carry more speed and let the bank do some of the leaning, but you still
pump and weight it. Choose an **outside–inside–outside** line to open the radius and carry speed;
time the apex to the corner's shape, delaying it when the exit is blind or the radius tightens.
- *Prereq:* COR-1..6.
- *Gear:* helmet; pads reasonable on faster berms.
- *Progression:* learn the assembled corner on bermed turns first (they're forgiving) → then flat
  turns (which demand real technique) → then off-camber (COR-8).
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: thetoliver.com (flat vs berm, O-I-O line, apex timing),
  promountainbike, WildSide (berms/flat/switchback/off-camber).

**COR-8 — OFF-CAMBER is the hardest corner — smooth inputs, no sudden braking, ride it or don't.**
The trail slopes away from the inside (toward the outside drop), so any rear-wheel traction loss
sends you over the edge. Keep the outside pedal weighted, inputs smooth (never jerky), look
through, and do NOT grab brakes suddenly (no grip to spare — BRK-4/BRK-7). The honest cue: ride
these smoothly or not at all.
- *Prereq:* COR-1..7, BRK-7 (surface reading).
- *Gear:* helmet; more protection where the consequence (the outside drop) is real.
- *Progression:* only after flat corners are solid; start on low-consequence off-camber before
  anything with exposure.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: DavidMTB (off-camber = hardest; smooth, don't grab brakes),
  WildSide, thetoliver.com.

## PROGRESSIONS (the organizing principle)

### The ladder (what depends on what)
- **Cornering sits on FND + BRK:** vision (FND-6/COR-1), bike-body separation (FND-7/COR-4),
  foot weighting (FND-5/COR-3), and the grip budget + brake-before (BRK-4/5/COR-2). A rider who
  can't yet brake-before-turn or hold a stable torso is **not ready** for speed in corners — fix
  the base first (diagnose bottom-up, per `coaching-methodology.md`).
- **Corner-type order: berm → flat → off-camber** (COR-7/8) — forgiving to demanding to
  high-consequence. Do not start a nervous rider on off-camber.

### Common errors → corrections (all Tier B-consensus)
- **Inside foot down / both pedals level** → drop and weight the OUTSIDE pedal (COR-3). Dramatic
  traction loss and pedal-strike risk otherwise.
- **Looking at the ground in front of the wheel** → eyes to the exit (COR-1); you're steering
  toward the inside without realizing it.
- **Braking in the corner** → finish braking on the approach, hands off at entry (COR-2); grabbing
  brakes mid-corner is a top cause of slide-outs.
- **Sitting up and letting the bike do all the leaning** → weight goes backward instead of through
  the tyre, costing grip; press into the corner and weight the outside foot (COR-3/4).
- **Full road-bike body-and-bike lean** → separate them: lean the bike more than the body (COR-4).

### How skills couple
- Vision (COR-1) chains up from FND-6 and forward into descending line choice (CLD).
- Grip budget (BRK-5) is *the* reason braking finishes before the lean.
- Bike-body separation (FND-7/COR-4) is the same skill that unlocks rough-terrain tracking (L2).
- Switchbacks = tight, often steep, often off-camber corners → belong to `terrain-features.md`
  (L2), built on COR-8 + steep-descent technique (CLD).

## Gaps
- **Corner-type depth is L1-thin** — berms (pumping the berm, high/low lines), true switchback
  technique (pivot vs wide line, foot-out), and drift/controlled-slide are **L2 gaps**. Switchbacks
  are cross-listed in `terrain-features.md` and need a dedicated progression.
- **No measured lean-angle / speed relationship** (COR-4 is qualitative) — L3 gap; a biomechanics
  or coach-with-data source would ground "how much lean for this radius/speed."
- **No wet/loose-condition cornering depth** (compound/pressure/line adaptations beyond "grippiest
  path") — L2 gap.
- **"Trail braking" into a corner** — flagged in `braking-traction.md` as an advanced, not-yet-
  laddered technique; it lands half here. Needs a Tier-A/B treatment before inclusion beyond a
  flagged note. **Do not teach as L1.**
- **No certification-body graded cornering standard** (BICP Minimum Ride Standard wording) —
  member-walled, L4 gap.
