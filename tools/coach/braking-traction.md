## How braking actually works

**BRK-1 — The front brake provides MOST of your stopping power; the rear assists — but grab a
handful of front and you go over the bars.** Under braking, weight transfers forward and loads the
front tyre, so the front brake has the most grip to work with. The skill is to *use* that power
without pitching over the bars: apply progressively, brace the weight shift (BRK-2), and never
stab the lever. On a firm surface, a common guide is **~twice as much front as rear**, tapering
toward balanced pressure for gentle slowing; use **less front as the surface gets loose** (BRK-4).
- *Prereq:* FND-1, FND-5 (heels down to brace).
- *Gear:* helmet; flat pedals make bracing feedback obvious while learning.
- *Progression:* on a firm, straight, gentle slope, apply *rear only* and feel weight go forward /
  rear go light → then *front only*, adding pressure each run until the rear feels like it wants to
  lift (that's peak firm-surface braking) → then combine, front-biased. Release instantly if a
  wheel skids.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: REI Techniques (*"Most of your braking power comes from your front
  brake, but grabbing a handful of front brake will send you over the bars"*); mtbtechniques.co.uk
  *Fundamentals/Braking* (the ~2:1 front:rear rule, adapt looser); evo/Fluidride (front ≈70% of
  stopping power but squeezed ~30% as hard as the rear).

**BRK-2 — PROGRESSIVE braking: squeeze like a dimmer, not a switch — and BRACE the weight shift.**
Apply the lever smoothly and increasingly, not with one firm pull. Progressive application lets the
front wheel *get weighted and gain grip* as force builds, rather than locking and skidding; it also
gives you feel for what's happening at the contact patch. As the bike slows, your body wants to
continue forward — extend the arms slightly, move the hips back a little, and drop the heels to
brace, so the bars are for balance and not support. *"Your brakes have a lever NOT a switch."*
- *Prereq:* FND-2, FND-5.
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Progression:* practise slow, smooth lever pressure on a gentle slope, feeling the fork settle →
  add the hip-back / heels-down brace → increase pressure only as the brace becomes automatic.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: mtbtechniques.co.uk (progressive braking, brace the shift,
  *"lever NOT a switch"*); promountainbike (*"Feather, don't grab"* — a locked wheel has less
  stopping force AND no direction).

**BRK-3 — Threshold, not lockup: a wheel at the edge of locking stops harder AND still steers.** A
locked wheel has almost no directional control and *less* braking force than a wheel modulated just
below lockup. If a wheel skids, **release** and re-apply with slightly less pressure. A sliding
**front** wheel especially: release the front, let it regain traction, recover direction — grabbing
harder is what turns a front washout into a crash.
- *Prereq:* BRK-1, BRK-2.
- *Gear:* helmet; consider more protection as you take this onto steeper/faster terrain.
- *Progression:* deliberately find rear lockup on a safe firm surface and practise the
  release-and-reapply → then front, very gently → build the reflex before speed.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: promountainbike (feather/threshold, front-washout recovery);
  mtbtechniques.co.uk (release on skid, re-apply lighter); BetterRide (front-brake control).

## Where and when to brake

**BRK-4 — Brake in a STRAIGHT LINE on GRIPPY surfaces, BEFORE the corner — not while turning.**
You have a finite grip budget at each tyre (BRK-5). Spend it on braking *or* cornering, not both:
scrub speed in a straight line on firm ground, release before you lean, and carry momentum through
the arc. Braking mid-corner risks a slide-out and prevents you leaning the bike. Avoid braking on
loose, off-camber, or slick surfaces (roots/wet rock) — it unbalances you and there's no grip to
spare. "Cut speed on smooth sections and coast through rough ones."
- *Prereq:* BRK-1..3, FND-6 (vision to see the corner early).
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Progression:* practise "brake before, hands off in the turn" on an easy corner (see COR
  chapter's four-phase corner) → progress to reading braking zones further ahead.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: REI (brake before the turn, momentum through); BikeRadar (scrub
  before corners, avoid braking mid-corner and on loose/off-camber); promountainbike/BetterRide
  (grip budget, don't brake off-camber). Couples into every corner (COR-2).

**BRK-6 — COVER the brakes on technical terrain — one finger, ready.** Rest one (disc) or two (rim)
fingers on the levers whenever terrain could demand a quick scrub, so the gap between "I need to
brake" and braking is zero. On fast technical ground that delay is the difference between control
and a crash. One finger is enough on modern discs and doesn't compromise grip on the bar. (Rim
brakes typically need two fingers.)
- *Prereq:* FND-1.
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Progression:* make covered-brakes the default hand position from the first ride, not a
  reach-when-needed motion.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: REI (index finger on levers 100% of the time; two for rim);
  BikeRadar (cover the anchors, one finger); promountainbike (cover on technical terrain).

## Traction & the grip budget

**BRK-5 — THE GRIP BUDGET: each tyre has a finite amount of grip, shared between braking and
turning.** A tyre can brake or turn near its limit, but not both at once — spend grip on one and
less is left for the other; overlap them and you exceed the limit and slide. This single idea is
*why* BRK-4 (brake before, not during, corners) is true, and why loading the tyre (heels down,
outside-foot weighting in corners) *buys* grip. Grip comes from two things: **weight** pushed into
the contact patch, and (in corners) **lean angle** engaging the tread. Loading the tyre from the
feet (FND-5) increases available grip; sitting up and unweighting it spends grip you didn't need to.
- *Prereq:* conceptual — pairs with FND-5 and BRK-4.
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Progression:* teach it as the *reason* behind braking and cornering drills, not as a standalone
  skill.
- *Tier:* B-consensus (mechanism). Source: promountainbike (contact-patch weight × lean angle;
  finite grip shared braking/cornering); WildSide cornering (*tires can brake or turn at full
  strength, but not both at once*); thetoliver.com (front-to-rear grip sharing). **Tier-C
  biomechanics grounding is a named gap below.**

**BRK-7 — Read the surface: match brake force and line to the grip actually available.** Big solid
rock and firm dirt give lots of grip (more front brake OK); loose gravel, wet roots, off-camber,
and slick rock give little (less front, brake earlier, or don't brake there at all). Spot grippy
patches to brake on and choose the cleanest, grippiest surface over the shortest line when traction
is limited.
- *Prereq:* BRK-1..5, FND-6.
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Progression:* practise identifying braking-safe vs braking-unsafe patches on a known trail
  before adding speed.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: mtbtechniques.co.uk (adapt front:rear to surface, brake on firmer
  sections); BetterRide (surface variables — sandstone vs roots vs loose rock); the cornering
  consensus (grippiest path over shortest).

## PROGRESSIONS (the organizing principle)

### The ladder (what depends on what)
- **Body position (FND-1/2/5) gates all braking** — you cannot brace braking forces or modulate a
  loaded front tyre without heels-down, heavy-feet, light-hands. Fix position before braking.
- **Progressive braking (BRK-2) → threshold control (BRK-3) → brake-then-turn (BRK-4)** is the
  intra-chapter order. Do not teach mid-corner braking control before straight-line threshold
  control is solid.
- **The grip budget (BRK-5) is the concept that ties braking to cornering** — it is the *why*
  under BRK-4 and under the cornering chapter's "brake before you lean."

### Common errors → corrections
- **Grabbing/stabbing the lever → lockup and skid** → progressive squeeze, dimmer not switch
  (BRK-2); release-and-reapply lighter on a skid (BRK-3).
- **Front-washout crash** → the crash is usually caused by grabbing *more* front as it slides;
  release the front to recover (BRK-3).
- **Braking mid-corner → slide-out** → move braking to the straight-line approach (BRK-4).
- **The "flying catapult":** straightening the arms and shoving the butt back to brace is an
  off-balance, end-of-range position — if the front then drops, you're launched forward. Brace by
  *bending* (hips back a little, heels down, arms with sag), not by locking out. *(This is the
  hinge to the ⚠ DISPUTED weight-back debate — see `climbing-descending.md` CLD.)*
  - *Tier:* B. Source: BetterRide (*the "flying catapult"*, weight-back-while-braking critique).

### How skills couple
- Braking control → **descending** (CLD): modulation on steeps is the same skill under more load.
- Grip budget (BRK-5) → **cornering** (COR): the corner is grip spent on turning, so braking must
  be finished first.
- Surface reading (BRK-7) → **line choice** everywhere.
