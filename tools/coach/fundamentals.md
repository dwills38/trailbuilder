# Fundamentals — Coach Corpus

**Maturity: foundation** (L1). Body position (neutral / ready-attack) · balance · vision ·
bike-body separation. Read [`INDEX.md`](INDEX.md) first (corpus rules, tiers, the SAFETY-FIRST

Fact IDs are stable and append-only (`FND-n`). Every skill fact carries **Prereq / Gear /

This chapter is the **base of the pyramid** — every other chapter's skills sit on top of these.
Per the PMBIA six-skill model, *position and balance* are the bottom of the pyramid and everything
above them fails if they're weak (see `coaching-methodology.md` CCH-1).

## Body position

**FND-1 — There are two base positions: NEUTRAL (efficient cruising) and READY / ATTACK
(technical terrain).** Neutral = rolling non-technical trail efficiently: pedals level and evenly
weighted, a *slight* bend in knees and elbows, index finger(s) covering the brakes, eyes ~15–20 ft
ahead. Ready/attack = trail gets steeper/rougher: **deep** bend in knees and elbows (~90°), rear
off the seat, hips shifted back, back flat and nearly parallel to the ground, still covering
brakes, still eyes 15–20 ft ahead. The rider transitions *between* them as terrain changes — they
are not two different riders, they are two ends of one dynamic range.
- *Prereq:* none — this is the base of the ladder.
- *Gear:* helmet minimum for all riding; flat pedals recommended while learning (they reward
  correct foot weighting — see FND-5).
- *Progression:* introduce neutral on flat ground → practise dropping into ready on a mild slope →
  cycle neutral↔ready repeatedly on rolling terrain until the transition is automatic.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: REI Expert Advice *Mountain Biking Techniques*
  (rei.com/learn/expert-advice/mountain-biking-techniques.html — the neutral/ready definitions);
  BikeRadar *How to master the attack position* (bikeradar.com/advice/skills/mtb-attack-position).

**FND-2 — The point of body position is to ISOLATE the core mass from the bike's chatter.** The
goal isn't a static pose; it's to keep head and torso (your core mass) smooth and balanced while
arms and legs work like suspension, letting the bike move and track the terrain underneath you.
*"Heavy feet, light hands"* — support your weight through your legs at the bottom bracket (low and
stable), not through your hands on the bar, so the arms are free for control and steering rather
than holding you up.
- *Prereq:* FND-1.
- *Gear:* helmet; a dropper post materially helps (drop the saddle so it's out of the way — FND-3).
- *Progression:* practise standing with heavy feet / light hands rolling a smooth trail → let the
  bike deflect off small bumps while keeping head/torso quiet → progressively rougher ground.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: BikeRadar attack-position (*"heavy feet, light hands"*, isolate the
  core mass, arms/legs as suspension); corroborated by promountainbike.com descending breakdown.

**FND-3 — Stand up on rough terrain; drop the saddle out of the way.** Sitting on rough ground
turns the bike into a bucking bronco and removes your legs from the suspension job. Stand, lower
the chest, keep the slight knee bend, and drop the saddle (dropper post) so it can't buck you or
block your range of motion. Sitting is for mild bumps and pedalling efficiency, not technical
terrain.
- *Prereq:* FND-1.
- *Gear:* dropper post strongly recommended (this is *why* the catalog treats droppers as core).
- *Progression:* on a mild descent, practise the same feature seated vs standing to feel the
  difference → default to standing as roughness increases.
- *Tier:* B. Source: BikeRadar attack-position.

## Balance & weight distribution

**FND-4 — The balance point is CENTRED over the bottom bracket; you MOVE around it as terrain
tilts.** On flat ground, weight is roughly even on both wheels with the core mass over the bottom
bracket. As terrain tilts, you shift *relative to the bike* to keep weight centred over the
contact patches: climbing → shift forward (keep the front down); descending → shift back (keep off
the bars). The bike tilts beneath you while your centre of gravity stays roughly constant over the
contact patches. Lowering the centre of gravity and getting *wider* on the bike increases
stability.
- *Prereq:* FND-1, FND-2.
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Progression:* find the centred/even-weight point on flat ground → introduce mild grade changes
  and practise the forward/back shift that *keeps* weight even → build to steeper grade changes
  (this is the seed of the climbing/descending chapter).
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: Liv Cycling *Uphill and Downhill body position*
  (liv-cycling.com/…/19049 — centred over BB, shift to stay balanced); bicyclenest.com body-position
  guide; corroborated by promountainbike descending breakdown. **See CLD chapter — the
  amount/direction of shift is gradient-dependent and, on steeps, ⚠ DISPUTED.**

**FND-5 — Drop the heels; weight the pedals through the feet.** Heels dropped below the pedal
spindle weights the pedals, lowers the centre of gravity, and stops the feet bouncing off the
pedals on impacts — and it directs body weight down through the cranks into the tyres (traction).
*"Think heavy feet."* Becomes automatic with practice.
- *Prereq:* FND-1.
- *Gear:* flat pedals make the feedback obvious; grippy shoes/pins help.
- *Progression:* practise the heels-down feel rolling smooth ground → hold it through small
  impacts → make it the default standing posture.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: BikeRadar (drop heels, weight low at BB); Liv Cycling (drop heels,
  brace against forces). Couples directly into braking (see `braking-traction.md` BRK-2).

## Vision

**FND-6 — Look where you WANT to go, ~15–20 ft ahead — the bike follows your eyes.** Vision is a
skill, not a passive act. Keep the chin up and eyes down the trail (roughly 15–20 ft on general
trail; further at speed, closer/slower when picking a technical line), scanning between "now" and
"next." Looking down at the front wheel unbalances you immediately and makes you react to terrain
instead of riding it. *"Look where you want to go, not where you don't."* This is the mechanism
behind cornering (COR) and descending (CLD): the eyes lead, the upper body follows the eyes, the
weight follows the upper body, the bike tracks the weight.
- *Prereq:* none (pairs with FND-1 from the very start).
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Progression:* on easy trail, consciously hold the gaze up-and-ahead → practise "spot the exit"
  on corners → scan now/next on rougher ground until it's automatic.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: REI Techniques (eyes 15–20 ft ahead, look where you want to go);
  BikeRadar (chin up, don't look at the front wheel); the cornering consensus (COR chapter) treats
  "eyes to the exit" as *mechanical fact*, not motivation.

## Bike-body separation

**FND-7 — The bike and body can move INDEPENDENTLY — this is the key that unlocks cornering and
rough terrain.** Rather than moving as one rigid unit, a skilled rider keeps the core mass stable
and *angles the bike* under it — leaning the bike more than the body in corners (COR), or letting
the bike deflect and track terrain while the torso stays quiet (FND-2). This "separation" is what
maintains traction: it keeps the centre of gravity over the contact patch while the bike does the
work of leaning or tracking.
- *Prereq:* FND-1, FND-2, FND-4.
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Progression:* introduce with slow figure-8s / cone weaves feeling the bike tip while the torso
  stays upright → develop in mellow berms → carry into flat and off-camber corners (COR chapter).
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: cornering consensus (thetoliver.com, DavidMTB — "bike-body
  separation," lean the bike more than the body). This is the FND-level statement of a skill the
  cornering chapter applies.

## PROGRESSIONS (the organizing principle)

*Prerequisite ladders, ordered steps, common errors + corrections, and how skills couple.*

### The ladder (what depends on what)
- **Position (FND-1) → Balance (FND-4) is the base of everything.** Per the PMBIA pyramid,
  position and balance are the bottom; braking, cornering, climbing/descending, and every L2
  maneuver sit on top and degrade if the base is weak. Diagnose a struggling rider from the bottom
  up (see `coaching-methodology.md`).
- **Vision (FND-6) is a parallel base rail** — introduced from day one, and the direct input to
  cornering ("eyes to the exit") and descending ("read terrain ahead, shift *before* you arrive").
- **Heavy feet / heels down (FND-2, FND-5) gate braking and traction** — you cannot brace braking
  forces or load the tyres for grip without them (BRK-2, and the grip budget in braking-traction).
- **Bike-body separation (FND-7) gates cornering** — flat and off-camber corners are unrideable at
  speed without it.

### Common errors → corrections
- **Sitting down on rough terrain** → stand, drop saddle, deep knee/elbow bend (FND-3). The single
  most common beginner error.
- **Supporting weight on the hands (stiff, straight arms)** → "heavy feet, light hands"; get weight
  low at the BB, free the arms (FND-2). Straight arms = no suspension range = the "flying
  catapult" failure under braking (see BRK chapter).
- **Looking at the front wheel** → chin up, eyes 15–20 ft ahead; it unbalances you and makes you
  reactive (FND-6).
- **Gripping the bars too tightly** → relaxed grip; tension kills the arms-as-suspension function
  and telegraphs into the core mass (FND-2).

### How skills couple (into later chapters)
- Vision → **cornering** (COR): the eyes lead the whole chain.
- Heels-down + heavy feet → **braking** (BRK-2) and **traction/grip budget**.
- Centred-and-shift (FND-4) → **climbing/descending** (CLD): the shift is gradient-driven.
- Bike-body separation (FND-7) → **cornering** (COR) and every L2 maneuver.
