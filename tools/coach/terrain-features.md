## The features ladder (build in this order, never skip rungs)

**TER-1 — The L2 maneuver ladder, lowest rung first.** Unchanged from the bootstrap seed — this
is the scaffold the rungs below now fill in: pumping → wheel lifts (front, then rear) → manual →
bunny hop → rolling/small drops → jumps (tabletop → gap). Alongside sit the terrain-reading
applications (rock gardens/roots, switchbacks, steeps) that reprioritize L1 skills rather than add
new airborne maneuvers.
- *Tier:* B-consensus scaffold. Source: `coaching-methodology.md` CCH-3/CCH-8; the L1 chapters.

### PUMPING

**TER-2 — Pumping = actively managing pressure through the bike to generate speed from terrain
shape, without pedalling.** The core mechanic: reduce pressure on faces pointing at you (the
uphill/front side of a bump) and increase pressure on faces pointing where you're going (the
downhill/back side) — "weight the scale" on the way down through a trough, unweight across a
crest. Arms manage the bike's *angle* (pull back through troughs, push forward across crests —
mandatory even at a beginner level, since it prevents an OTB); legs manage *elevation* (absorb the
front of a bump, extend/press into the back of it). A quiet, level torso is the target — the bike
moves under a stable rider, not the other way round.
- *Prereq:* FND-1/2 (neutral/ready position), FND-5 (heavy feet).
- *Gear:* helmet; a pump track or smooth rollers to learn on (any bike works, though a hardtail
  or short-travel bike gives more direct feedback than a long-travel full-suspension rig).
- *Progression:* introduce arms-only bar management on rollers first (prevents OTB, the safety
  floor) → develop leg-driven pressure on the same rollers → commit to combined arm+leg "row" on
  troughs and "anti-row" on crests once each half is separately solid. Practise on a pump track or
  rollers before applying to trail bumps at speed.
- *Common errors → corrections:* stiff arms/no bar management on a bump → OTB risk; start here,
  not with leg power. Pedalling through pump sections instead of pumping → wastes the trail's free
  speed and kills flow; the whole point is generating speed *without* pedalling.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: BikeRadar (*How to use pump bumps to gain speed* — approach in
  control, absorb the upslope, push down the downslope, repeat), Mountain Bike Action / RideLogic
  Master Coach Patrick Carey (arms-manage-angle / legs-manage-elevation breakdown, the mandatory
  arm-bar-management-prevents-OTB point), Pinkbike / Taj Mihelich (the "weight the scale" down-pump
  / up-pump framing, corners as a sideways pump).

### WHEEL LIFTS (front, then rear)

**TER-3 — Front wheel lift: "Neutral, Load, Explode."** From a neutral, coasting, controlled-speed
position, pre-load by getting tall and stomping down through the pedals/bottom bracket — the
bigger the compression, the bigger the rebound. As the bike rebounds, pull the handlebars up
toward the chest to lift the front wheel. This is the foundation rung for manuals, bunny hops, and
drop control (front-wheel management on landing) — nothing above it can be taught safely first.
- *Prereq:* FND-1/2 (neutral position), TER-2 (pumping/compression feel).
- *Gear:* helmet; flat, open ground, no obstacles for the first reps.
- *Progression:* introduce the compress-and-rebound motion through a lane of cones with **no**
  obstacles → develop timing against a line on the ground → commit to lifting over a real small
  obstacle only once the motion is consistent without one.
- *Common errors → corrections:* pulling with the arms before compressing → weak, arm-only lift
  with poor height; the compression (legs/bottom bracket) must come first, the pull is a top-off,
  not the source.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: Mountain Bike Training Center (front/rear/level/sequential wheel
  lift breakdown), the VVWF Coaching Manual (the "Neutral, Load, Explode" cue, the pre-load →
  rebound mechanism).

**TER-4 — Rear wheel lift: the "donkey kick" scoop, timed to the same pre-load rebound.** Same
neutral-position stomp/pre-load as the front lift, but instead of pulling the bars, the feet
"scoop" back and up (the donkey-kick motion) as the chest leans forward to unweight the rear tyre;
weight shifts into the handlebars to create tension between pedals and bars so the feet have
something to push against. Useful on its own for repositioning the rear wheel in tight
switchbacks, not just for clearing step-ups.
- *Prereq:* TER-3 (front lift; same pre-load mechanic), FND-5 (foot weighting).
- *Gear:* helmet; open ground, cones, no obstacle first.
- *Progression:* introduce the pre-load + scoop motion through cones with no obstacle → develop
  timing → commit over a real obstacle. **Keep one finger covering the rear brake throughout** —
  if a lift becomes an unintended over-rotation ("loop-out" risk direction reversed for the rear
  lift is less severe than a manual loop-out, but the brake habit should be built here anyway).
- *Common errors → corrections:* trying to "kick" without the initial stomp/pre-load → the lift
  has no power source; the donkey-kick only works as the second half of a pre-load rebound, not
  as an isolated arm/leg flick.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: Mountain Bike Training Center, VVWF Coaching Manual (rear wheel
  lift "what/why/how," the donkey-kick description, sequential- and level-lift variants as
  higher-speed combinations of the same two skills).

### MANUAL

**TER-5 — The manual: preload-and-punch to the balance point over the rear hub, held by the
hips/legs, not the arms.** A manual is a standing wheelie with **no pedalling** — momentum and
body weight alone keep the front wheel up. The initiation is the same two-part sequence as a
wheel lift, described by coaches as **"preload and punch"**: compress down into the bottom
bracket, then explode the hips backward toward (or behind) the rear axle while the arms push the
bars forward and stay straight (a rowing motion, not a pull — pulling up with the arms drives the
chest over the bars and crashes the front wheel back down). Once up, the balance point is held
with the **legs** (extend/hips-back if the front drops, bend/hips-forward if it's rising too far)
while the **arms stay locked straight** throughout; side-to-side balance is corrected with the
knees/hips, not the bars. **Cover the rear brake at all times** — a light tap instantly drops the
front wheel and is the standard, coach-endorsed correction for an over-rotation ("loop-out"), not
a failure.
- *Prereq:* TER-3 (front wheel lift — same preload mechanic), FND-4 (balance), TER-2 (pumping/
  compression feel).
- *Gear:* helmet; flat pedals recommended while learning (easier bail); lowest saddle position;
  open, grippy, flat ground with a runout — first sessions on a gentle grass slope so an
  intentional over-rotation can be bailed safely (see progression below).
- *Progression:* **introduce** the fail-safe first — on a grassy slope, deliberately pull past the
  balance point and step off the back until doing so feels automatic (this removes the fear that
  otherwise causes riders to half-commit and never reach the balance point) → **develop** the
  preload-and-punch on flat ground without trying to hold the balance point yet, just getting the
  front wheel up and immediately covering the rear brake → **commit** to holding the balance point
  for longer stretches, then to rolling it over small trail features (rollers, shallow holes) once
  balance is consistent on flat ground.
- *Common errors → corrections:* pulling up with the arms instead of driving the hips back → chest
  collapses over the bars, front wheel drops; keep arms straight, drive with hips/legs. Sitting
  the saddle high → physically blocks the hips from reaching the balance point; drop it fully.
  Fear-driven half-commitment → do the intentional-bail drill on grass before attempting a real
  balance-point hold.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: REI Expert Advice (preload/punch, "it's all in the hips," cover the
  rear brake, lower the seat), Red Bull (*5 essential tips*: hips not arms, straight arms/bent
  legs, rear-brake safety net, seat height), Mountain Bike Action (balance point over the rear hub,
  hips as counterweight, front wheel ~1 foot up as a starting target), bike198.com (the
  preload-and-punch terminology, the grassy-slope bail drill, the rowing-motion arm cue).

### BUNNY HOP

**TER-6 — Bunny hop = a pumped manual (front wheel up) immediately followed by scooping the rear
wheel up — two separately-mastered skills stacked, front then rear, never simultaneous for a
beginner.** Coaches converge on the "American" bunny hop (front wheel up first, then rear scooped
behind) as the technique to learn — it works with flat OR clipless pedals and builds real skill,
unlike the clipless-only "English" hop that can foster the bad habit of using pedal float to lift
the rear. Sequence: (1) the pumped manual — compress, then shift weight over the rear wheel as the
suspension rebounds, minimizing arm pull; (2) as the front reaches height, stand tall, which shifts
weight forward; (3) throw the bars forward to bring the rear wheel up while scooping the pedals
upward with the feet and bending the legs to absorb the bike into the body; (4) land front wheel
first (or both together), soaking up the impact with bent arms/legs, staying off the brakes.
- *Prereq:* TER-3 + TER-4 (front and rear wheel lifts — a bunny hop is not a new motion, it's the
  two lifts sequenced together), TER-5 recommended (the pumped-manual entry is literally a manual).
- *Gear:* helmet; flat pedals while learning (easier to escape, avoids the pedal-assisted-hop
  crutch); lowered saddle.
- *Progression:* **introduce** each wheel's motion separately against a line on the ground (no
  obstacle) — front-wheel timing first, then rear — until both wheels clear the line together at
  low speed → **develop** by adding a small stick/obstacle only once the line drill is consistent →
  **commit** to trail obstacles (roots, logs, puddles) starting small and building up. "Start small
  and build up" is explicit in every source — this is not a skill to learn on a trail-sized log.
- *Common errors → corrections:* lifting front and rear simultaneously as a beginner → loses the
  height and control that sequencing gives; lift front first, always. Using the rear brake to
  "help" the rear come up → not a substitute for the scoop technique. Landing rear-wheel-first →
  can wash out the front; land front first or level.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: BikeRadar (5-step "pumped manual" bunny hop guide, the front-then-
  rear sequencing, weight-transfer timing), Ninja Mountain Bike Skills / ridelikeaninja.com (9-step
  guide, explicit "start small and build up," compression-then-rise-then-clearance sequence),
  Canadian Cycling Magazine / Coach Peter Glassford (front wheel first, not simultaneous — a named-
  coach source independently confirming the sequencing).

### ROLLING → SMALL COMMITTED DROPS

**TER-7 — Drops: ready position first (rollable), then the front-wheel "poke," then progressively
bigger with a front wheel lift added, always with a scouted/known runout.** Coaches place drops
second only to jumps in lesson frequency, and stress an explicit **progression of difficulty**
rather than a single technique:
1. **Rollable drops** (both wheels can stay on the ground) — approach in the ready/neutral
   position, roll over the top under control; no boosting or jumping needed.
2. **The front-tire "poke"** — as the front wheel crosses the lip, push the handlebar *forward*
   (not up/pull) so the front tyre moves forward relative to the rider, shifting the centre of mass
   back toward the rear wheel; this is the technique for small (roughly sub-2-foot, non-rollable)
   drops.
3. **Intermediate drops (~2 ft+, non-rollable)** — add a **front wheel lift** (TER-3) before the
   lip: the lift pushes the bike further from the lip, giving the rear tyre time to follow before
   the front drops, and helps the bike land level instead of nose-first.
4. **Bigger/advanced drops** — the same motions become more exaggerated and dynamic; "lead the
   front wheel down" with the arms as it clears the lip (rather than just poking/pushing forward)
   to stay neutral rather than pitching over the rear wheel; press down through feet and hands to
   compress the bike before leaving the lip, especially at low speed (less press is needed as speed
   increases); absorb the landing by bending elbows/knees as the rear wheel touches down first.
- *Prereq:* ready/neutral position (FND-1/2), TER-3 (front wheel lift) for intermediate+ drops,
  BRK-4/BRK-7 (brake-before / 2-1-0 braking discipline — never brake hard immediately on landing,
  never grab the front brake going over the lip or on landing).
- *Gear:* helmet always; knee pads from the rollable stage on (real consequence starts small);
  full-face + more protection as drop height and landing consequence increase. **Always walk/scout
  the feature and confirm a clean, known runout before riding it** — this is a repeated, explicit
  safety point across sources, especially once landings become blind.
- *Progression:* **introduce** on a flat trail/curb-height rollable drop, both wheels down, no air
  → **develop** the front-tyre poke on small (sub-2-ft) non-rollable drops with a known flat runout
  → **commit** to intermediate drops (~2 ft+) adding the front wheel lift, then to advanced drops
  only at a bike park or maintained progression feature (a "ladder drop" that lets a rider choose
  height incrementally is the coach-recommended tool for this stage) — never scale up height and
  new terrain on the same session. Never ride a drop with an unscouted/blind landing.
- *Common errors → corrections:* grabbing the front brake going over the lip or immediately on
  landing → pitches the rider over the bars; brake before the feature (2-1-0: both brakes on
  approach, rear-only if needed over the obstacle, zero brakes on exit), never on the lip or
  landing. Pushing/poking the bike too far forward → over-rotates weight onto the rear wheel and
  loses front-end control on landing; the poke is a small forward shift, not a full manual yank.
  Nose-diving on landing → usually too little rear-weight shift or too much forward lean; keep
  chest up, absorb with bent arms/legs, land front-and-rear as close to together as the drop's
  geometry allows.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: Bicycling.com / MTB Skills Factory owner Josh Whitmore + PMBIA-L1/
  USAC-L1 coach Jane Marshall (the ready-position → poke → front-wheel-lift progression, the 2-1-0
  braking principle, scouting blind landings), BetterRide / Gene Hamilton (core-skills-first
  framing, the "ride off it in balance, in control, in neutral" baseline, tabletop-before-doubles
  equivalent for drops, "baby step your way up," look past the landing not at it), GMBN / coach
  Becci Skelton (a ladder-drop progression tool, walking the feature to visualize, reducing entry
  speed and braking earlier when a drop feels "hot").

### JUMPS (tabletop → gap) — the highest-injury-risk rung

**TER-8 — Jumps: approach the takeoff with speed set beforehand (not by pedalling into the lip),
preload into the transition, "pop" (a bunny-hop-like extension) timed to the lip, then a relaxed
airborne phase and a two-wheels-together landing absorbed with the arms and legs — start on
tabletops, never doubles.** Every source insists jumping starts on a **tabletop** (a jump whose
landing ramp is connected to the takeoff by dirt, so a short landing is still rollable) rather
than a **double** (a true gap where a short landing means casing into a flat or upslope with no
mercy) — tabletops give "maximum room for error." The mechanic, consistent across sources:
1. **Approach** — get speed before the transition; stop pedalling as you enter it. Assume the
   attack/ready position, pedals level, weight central.
2. **Preload** — as the front wheel starts up the transition, compress the body/suspension (a
   squat-jump analogy); the compression should bottom out and begin extending so full extension
   lands right at the lip.
3. **Pop** — the extension through the lip is mechanically a bunny hop: pull through the bars,
   extend the body, staying centered rather than drifting nose-high. Treat the front and rear
   wheel as separate events (front first, then rear), exactly as in a bunny hop — this is *less*
   explosive than a bunny hop because the lip itself supplies lift the wheels would otherwise need
   from a hop.
4. **Air** — relax, stay loose (a stiff rider gets bucked by the landing), look at the landing, not
   the sky or the front wheel. Optionally give yourself a small task (e.g. a subtle bar turn) to
   stay engaged rather than "holding on for dear life" — an unfocused rider in the air is a rider
   who over-corrects on touchdown.
5. **Landing** — match the bike's angle to the slope of the landing ("nose the bike into
   transition"); land both wheels together (or very close) with arms/legs absorbing the impact;
   stay off the brakes until fully settled and rolling.
- *Prereq:* pumping (TER-2, for the takeoff mechanics), the full wheel-lift → manual → bunny hop
  ladder (TER-3–TER-6 — the pop **is** a bunny hop; a rider who can't bunny hop reliably on flat
  ground is not ready to jump), a committed ready/attack position (FND-1/2), drop landing control
  (TER-7, for absorbing an airborne landing).
- *Gear:* helmet always; knee/elbow pads from first tabletop attempts; full-face for anything with
  real height, speed, or park/DH-style features (cross-ref `discipline-craft.md` DSC-5). Flat
  pedals strongly preferred while learning (a clipless pedal habit of yanking the bike up with the
  feet is explicitly called out as a bad habit to avoid). Practice ground: a **tabletop with a
  shallow, non-steep takeoff** — not a steep dirt-jump-style lip, not a double.
- *Progression:* **introduce** on the smallest available tabletop, ridden slowly, focusing only on
  staying centered/neutral through the arc (visualize riding over an invisible dome rather than
  truly leaving the ground) → **develop** by slowly increasing speed until both wheels land
  together or slightly front-first → **commit** to bigger tabletops one increment at a time, and
  only consider a double once every available tabletop size is genuinely comfortable. Tabletop
  style ("style" tricks such as a full tabletop body position) and true gap jumps are both
  explicitly *later* stages that assume the basic jump is already dialed — do not layer style or
  gaps onto an unproven takeoff/landing.
- *Common errors → corrections (the never-case / never-dead-sailor safety content):* pedalling
  into the takeoff → removes the precise timing the pop needs and risks pedal strikes; set speed
  before the transition and stop pedalling. Pulling up too early → wastes the preload and can come
  up short (undershoot/"casing" the landing — the single most consequential jump mistake, since a
  short landing on a double has no forgiving dirt ramp). Leaning back "for safety" in the air →
  actually puts the rider off-balance and behind the bike's trajectory; stay centered. Still
  pushing through the arms/pressing the lip past the transfer point → gets the rider "bucked"
  forward over the bars — the weight-transfer line (from hands to feet) has to be complete before
  the lip, and moves further back as jump size/suspension travel increases. Grabbing brakes on
  landing → destabilizes an already-loaded bike; stay off the brakes until settled.
- *Tier:* B-consensus (the strongest corroboration in this chapter — five independent named-coach
  sources agree on sequencing and the tabletop-first safety rule). Source: BikeRadar (coach Sam of
  Pedal Progression + MBUK's Will Soffe: tabletops-first, front/rear-as-separate-events, the
  weight-transfer line, land-both-wheels), REI Expert Advice (manual→bunny hop→jump explicit
  prerequisite chain, preload/pop terminology, matching tyres to landing slope), BetterRide / Gene
  Hamilton (core-skills-before-airtime framing, tabletop-without-steep-takeoff selection, "baby
  step," look past the landing), Canadian Cycling Magazine / BMX coach Barry Nobles (speed-before-
  the-lip not pedalling-into-it, preload-as-squat-jump, "give yourself a task in the air" for
  control), BikeRadar tabletop-style follow-up (style/gap jumps as an explicitly later stage once
  basic jumping and comfort with bigger jumps are already established).

### ROCK GARDENS & ROOTS

**TER-8b — Rock gardens/roots: scout the line from the exit backward, set speed before entering
(not inside), then stay centered with "heavy feet, light hands" and let the bike move underneath a
quiet upper body.** This is an L1-skills-at-speed application, not a new airborne maneuver, but the
consequence of getting body position or vision wrong compounds fast on chattery terrain — DH World
Cup bobbles/crashes "almost always" trace back to a breakdown of body position or vision per a
named coach source. Key points, corroborated across independent coach sites:
- **Scout before riding it** — walk the section, ideally from the exit backward (it's easier to
  know where the bike must end up than to guess forward from the entrance); identify the "disruptor"
  rocks/roots (square edges, grabbers, anything long enough to hit the bottom bracket) and plan to
  avoid looking at them directly (vision leads the bike, COR-1's mechanism applied here too).
- **Braking finishes before the rocks start**, not inside them — braking mid-section makes the
  fork ride lower and pings the front tyre off edges instead of rolling cleanly; this is the
  grip-budget principle (BRK-5) applied to chatter, not just to corners.
- **Body position: heavy feet, light hands, low chest, soft elbows** — pressure through the feet
  keeps the front end free to track over obstacles; a rider who gets yanked around by every impact
  is too stiff or too far forward.
- **Roots and rocks, crossed at an angle, are the classic slip trap** — cross perpendicular where
  possible, especially wet; unweight the front wheel slightly over a large root/rock so it floats
  rather than slams, then shift weight forward so the rear follows smoothly.
- *Prereq:* solid ready position (FND-1/2), heavy-feet weighting (FND-5), vision (COR-1/BRK-7
  surface reading), brake-before discipline (BRK-4/5).
- *Gear:* helmet; knee pads reasonable given the terrain's unpredictability; consider slightly
  higher rear tyre pressure and, for consequential riding, a tyre insert (a named coach's personal
  setup choice, not a universal rule).
- *Progression:* introduce on a short, low-consequence rock garden — walk it, ride it slow first
  ("one test run too slow," per one source's session plan) → develop by adding controlled speed in
  small increments, one variable at a time (line, then speed, then body tension) → commit to
  full-speed technical sections only once a slower pass through the same section is clean and
  repeatable. Change only one variable per session block.
- *Common errors → corrections:* looking at the obstacle you're trying to avoid → you'll hit it
  (the same COR-1 mechanism); look at the next calm patch. Braking inside the rock garden → harsher,
  less accurate bike; do the braking beforehand. Crossing roots/rocks at an angle → slip risk,
  worse wet; cross perpendicular. Stiff arms fighting every impact → loosen hands/elbows so the
  chassis can move under a stable torso.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: Singletracks / Jeremiah "Scratch" Stone (Ninja MTB Performance
  instructor — body position, disruptor-rock identification, ratcheting/track-stand for momentum
  loss, "downhill rock gardens are all about body position and eyes ahead"), Mountain Bike Action
  (scout on foot from exit backward, watch other riders, eyes up not on the front wheel),
  bicyclenest.com (perpendicular root/rock crossing, front-wheel unweighting technique, wet-
  condition adjustments) — three independent sources agreeing on scouting + vision + perpendicular
  crossing; a fourth session-plan source corroborates the pre-section braking point and the
  one-variable-at-a-time practice structure.

**TER-9 — Switchbacks = off-camber cornering (COR-8) meets steep-descent technique (CLD-5) —
now has a dedicated progression in `cornering.md` COR-10.** Round 2 (2026-07-17) fetched and
laddered dedicated switchback technique (uphill/downhill/level distinctions, wide-entry geometry,
shoulder-led rotation, drills) as `cornering.md` COR-10 — this fact is now a pointer, not a gap.
The rear wheel lift (TER-4) is confirmed directly applicable to tight switchbacks (repositioning
the rear wheel), per COR-10's prerequisites. The extreme-tightness pivot family (stoppie
pivot / nose pivot / cutty) is separately flagged in `cornering.md` COR-10b as NOT laddered
(blocked on an unsourced stoppie-control prerequisite) — do not teach it from this cross-reference.
- *Prereq:* COR-8, CLD-5, TER-4 (rear wheel lift).
- *Tier:* B-consensus — see `cornering.md` COR-10 for the full citation set.

**TER-10 — Steeps & chutes: bend forward from the hips (not the waist) to keep weight through the
bottom bracket, shift braking bias hard toward the rear as gradient increases, scout and commit
fully — no half-measures.** Round 3 (2026-07-18) fetches the committed-rung technique flagged as a
gap since the bootstrap round, closing it. **Body position:** bend forward at the hips as the front
wheel drops into the chute — counterintuitive, but it lowers the chest and keeps useful bend in the
arms rather than straightening them; legs stay perpendicular to *flat* ground (not to the slope),
which keeps weight running through the bottom bracket and cranks rather than being thrown onto the
hands. Adjust how far forward you lean to the steepness of the specific slope, not a fixed angle.
**Braking bias inverts as gradient increases:** minimize front-brake use on steep terrain — grabbing
front brake here unbalances the bike (worse on loose/rutted ground) and can drag the front wheel
out of a rut — and shift toward the rear brake instead; independently corroborated by a named World
Cup DH/Olympic-level source: "the steeper the trail, the more you want to use your rear brake and
shift your weight back over the rear tire to maintain pressure and traction... feather and minimize
your front-brake use. The last thing you want is to lock up the front wheel on the steepest
sections." This is a *sustained bias for the duration of the steep section*, distinct from
`discipline-craft.md` DSC-9's maximal-braking technique, which is a short, deliberate burst used to
shed speed before or between steep pitches, not a riding position held throughout one. **Undulation
management:** squash (let the bike come up into you) over rises and pump (press the bike into the
terrain) through troughs while riding the chute — the same TER-2 pumping mechanic applied to a
steep, committed line rather than a rolling one. **Commitment:** braking is finished *before*
dropping in wherever the terrain allows ("once you enter a steep section, it can be too late to
adjust speed"), and the honest, repeated coaching cue across sources is to commit fully once
you've started — hesitating or breaking the move up mid-chute is explicitly named as the failure
mode, not a safer middle ground. On chutes with more direction-change than a straight fall line,
prefer the entry/line that minimizes changes in direction, and use any shallower, grippier ground
before and after the steep pitch to set up and regain control rather than trying to adjust mid-
chute.
- *Common errors → corrections:* "hanging off the back" (sitting far back with straight arms) →
  named explicitly as counterproductive: it "gives you zero front wheel grip, bucks you forward if
  you hit a bump, and gives you the least amount of stability" — bend from the hips and keep weight
  through the bottom bracket instead of retreating behind it. Grabbing front brake on the steepest
  ground → locks the front wheel with no steering left; feather rear-biased braking instead. Half-
  committing or breaking the move up partway through → increases risk rather than reducing it; the
  coaching consensus is scout fully, then commit fully.
- *Prereq:* CLD-5 (steep-descent weighting — this is CLD-5's committed extreme), BRK-3 (threshold/
  release-and-reapply — the rear-bias braking still needs threshold control, not a stomp), TER-2
  (pumping, for the squash/pump-through-undulations layer), FND-1/2 (ready position, heavy feet).
- *Gear:* full-face/more protection as consequence increases (cross-ref `discipline-craft.md`
  DSC-4's gear-escalation note); a dropper post fully down; always scout the chute's entry, line,
  and runout before committing, especially where the exit or landing is blind.
- *Progression:* **introduce** the forward-hip-bend/legs-perpendicular-to-flat-ground position and
  the front-to-rear braking-bias shift on a short, low-consequence steep pitch with a known, open
  runout → **develop** by adding the squash/pump undulation-management layer once the base position
  and braking bias are automatic → **commit** to longer, more consequential chutes only once the

## PROGRESSIONS (the organizing principle)

### The ladder (what depends on what) — the whole point of this chapter
- **Everything here sits on solid L1.** A rider whose body position, braking, or vision is shaky is
  **not ready** for any L2 feature — the coach fixes the base first (CCH-1 pyramid, diagnose
  bottom-up).
- **The vertical ladder is now fully specified end to end:** pumping (TER-2) → front wheel lift
  (TER-3) → rear wheel lift (TER-4) → manual (TER-5) → bunny hop (TER-6, literally a pumped manual
  + rear scoop) → rolling/small drops (TER-7, uses the front wheel lift for landing control) →
  jumps (TER-8, whose "pop" phase **is** a bunny hop). Every rung's Progression section is built on
  the rung(s) below it by name, not just by cross-reference — this is the strongest structural
  guarantee against skipping rungs that the corpus can offer short of live coaching.
- **Jumps (TER-8) carry the highest injury risk in the whole corpus.** The never-case /
  never-dead-sailor content (undershoot risk, the weight-transfer-line timing, tabletop-before-
  double) is now sourced and explicit, not just flagged — this closes the chapter's #1 listed gap
  from the bootstrap round.
- **Gear escalates with the rung:** helmet always; knee pads from wheel lifts/manuals onward; more
  protection (full-face) as drops/jumps/steeps become consequential or move toward park/DH terrain
  (cross-ref `discipline-craft.md`).

### How this chapter couples
- Pumping (TER-2) → cornering (pumping berms, COR-7) and jumping (TER-8's preload/pop mechanics
  are literally pump mechanics applied to a lip).
- Wheel lifts (TER-3/4) → manual (TER-5, same preload mechanic), drops (TER-7, front-wheel landing
  control), switchbacks (TER-9, rear-wheel repositioning), technical climbing (step-ups).
- Manual (TER-5) + wheel lifts → bunny hop (TER-6) → jump pop phase (TER-8).
- Rock gardens/roots (TER-8b) → cornering vision (COR-1) and the grip budget (BRK-5), applied to
  chatter instead of a single corner.
- Steeps/switchbacks → the descending and cornering chapters (their parent skills). Steeps
  specifically (TER-10) → `discipline-craft.md` DSC-9 (the DH-specific maximal-braking burst
  technique, a distinct sibling to TER-10's sustained rear-brake-bias position) and DSC-4 (calm-
  ground braking-point discipline, the flatter-terrain counterpart).

### Common errors across the whole ladder (cross-maneuver patterns)
- **Arm-only lifting/pulling** (wheel lifts, manual, bunny hop, jump pop) is the single most
  repeated correction across sources: the power source is always the legs/hips compressing and
  driving, with the arms guiding/rowing, never yanking. A coach diagnosing a stuck rider at *any*
  rung should check this first.
- **Braking inside a feature instead of before it** (drops, rock gardens) recurs as a
  cross-maneuver fault — set speed on the approach, release/finish braking before the technical
  section, per the 2-1-0 principle (TER-7) and the rock-garden braking-point discipline (TER-8b).
- **Skipping straight to a bigger version of a feature** (a bigger drop, a double instead of a
  tabletop, real speed before a slow clean pass) is named explicitly as the failure mode across
  drops, jumps, and rock gardens — every source's progression is incremental for a reason.

## Wet & loose conditions on jumps and drops (round 5, 2026-07-18 — partial close, read the limits)

**TER-14 — In the wet, grip splits into two DIFFERENT things a rider can trade between — rubber
friction and mechanical (knob-into-ground) grip — and the correct trade depends on whether the
surface is firm-and-wet or soft-and-muddy. For jumps and drops specifically, the sourced adjustments
are all about the APPROACH and the RUN-OUT, not about changing the maneuver in the air.**
The chapter's Gaps flagged "wet/loose-surface adjustments for jumps and drops specifically" as an
open L2 gap. This closes the part that public sources actually support, and states plainly what they
do not — because inventing a wet-jump cue is exactly the failure mode this corpus exists to avoid.

*The grip model to teach (this is the useful, transferable part).* Water forms a thin layer that
cuts **friction** — the rubber-on-surface component. But grip is not only friction: **mechanical
grip** comes from the tread grabbing edges or digging into soft ground. The two respond oppositely
to rubber compound, so the right choice is surface-dependent:
- **Wet on FIRM ground** (slick rock, wet hardpack, wet roots): friction is what you lost, so you
  want it back — an intermediate/dry tread with a **softer** compound and slightly lower pressure
  generates more friction. Shorter, wider knobs maximize contact.
- **Wet on SOFT ground** (mud): friction is nearly irrelevant; you need to **dig**. A wet-condition
  tyre with **taller, narrower** knobs and a firmer rubber penetrates and sheds mud, generating
  mechanical grip. Softer rubber can deform too much to bite.
  This is the tyre-side twin of COR-18's finding that on deformable terrain grip comes from shearing
  the ground rather than from friction.

*Sourced technique for wet/loose approaches and landings:*
- **Link grip points, don't fight the whole trail.** The named wet-riding method is to stop seeing
  obstacles and start seeing the grippy bits, then **stay low and drive weight into the parts of the
  trail you can trust** — turning "twenty negative connections into three deliberately positive
  ones." Applied to a feature: choose where you set up, brake and press based on where grip *is*.
- **Heavy / light / heavy over the slippery bit.** On wet roots, the honest sourced position is that
  you *cannot* stop the tyre slipping — roots are slippery and there will be movement. What you can
  do is get low, **generate weight against the trail either side of the root, go light across it, and
  go heavy again after** — taking control back where grip is trustworthy. This is directly applicable
  to a wet lip or a wet run-in crossed by roots.
- **Undershoot rather than overshoot, always — and more so in the wet.** A bike-park coaching source
  states it as a rule that holds at every level, up to slopestyle: it is "always safer and more
  controlled to undershoot a jump than to overshoot." Wet conditions reduce approach speed
  predictability, which makes conservative speed the correct default.
- **The run-out is the wet hazard, not the landing.** Most drop crashes "don't actually happen on the
  drop, they happen AFTER" — you are travelling faster on landing than on takeoff, and in the wet
  that is where reduced braking grip bites. Look **past** the landing to where you want to go, and
  inspect the run-out before committing.
- **Do not grab brakes on touchdown** (already TER-6/TER-7's rule) — the consequence is simply larger
  in the wet, where a skid starts sooner and recovers later.
- **Setup for wet:** sintered brake pads generally perform better wet; reduce spring rate and quicken
  rebound slightly (wet speeds and forces are lower, so the same settings ride harsh and skip);
  reduce compression damping to improve low-speed traction; full-length mudguard and eye protection
  are a visibility requirement, not a comfort item. Record any change so it can be reversed.
- **⚠ The confidence trap, named by two independent sources.** Wet riding fails through *tentative*
  riding — moving "weakly and hesitantly," which unloads the tyres exactly when they need weight. A
  reader-reported pattern worth teaching: the first set of wet roots is ridden fine, then the rider
  notices them, becomes careful, and crashes. The coaching answer is **not** "be brave" — it is
  CCH-16's stop rule (if it isn't a full yes, don't) combined with dropping *back down* the ladder to
  a feature the rider can commit to fully in these conditions. **A rider who is going to ride a
  feature tentatively should ride a smaller feature instead.**

- *⚠ HONEST LIMIT — what this fact does NOT establish.* No public source located this round gives
  **wet-specific takeoff or in-air technique for jumps** — how a wet lip changes the press/pop
  timing, whether a wet takeoff can slip under load, or how to adjust the extend-vs-absorb choice in
  the wet. Everything above concerns approach, grip selection, speed choice, run-out and setup. The
  in-air phase is, mechanically, unaffected by surface once the tyres leave the ground — but the
  *takeoff loading* phase plausibly is, and **no source was found that addresses it**, so this corpus
  does not. Per rule 8 the honest entry is: not established. What would settle it — a
  certification-body wet/winter air module, or a named coach's wet bike-park breakdown.
- *Tier:* B / B-consensus for the grip model and the wet-riding method (multiple independent
  method-serious sources); B for the individual feature cues. Sources: Ben Cathro (former World Cup
  DH racer), *How NOT to Bike — How To Not Suck When Riding In The Wet*, Pinkbike (2024) — the
  friction-vs-mechanical-grip model, compound/tread trade by surface, brake-pad, suspension and
  mudguard adjustments, and the tentativeness failure mode; Andy Barlow (Dirt School), *How to ride
  wet roots and slippery trails*, MBR (2022) — grip-point linking, heavy/light/heavy, stay-low-and-use-
  your-knees (already the corpus's BRK-13 source); Highland Mountain Bike Park, *Progression Guide —
  How To Jump* (2025) — the undershoot-over-overshoot rule and the roll → pop-to-knuckle → build-speed
  session progression; BetterRide (Gene Hamilton), *How to Hit Jumps and Drops at Any Age* — crashes
  happen after the drop, look past the landing, inspect blind landings. Fetched/search-highlighted
  2026-07-18.
- *⚠ SAFETY-FIRST:* this fact **narrows** the envelope of TER-4..TER-7; it never widens it. Wet
  conditions are a reason to drop a rung, not a technique for holding the same rung. The gear
  assumptions of the underlying dry progressions are unchanged and still apply.
- *Corpus use:* pairs with `braking-traction.md` BRK-13 (low-traction braking) and `cornering.md`
  COR-17/COR-18 (low-grip cornering and its mechanism); supplies the conditions layer the drop/jump

## Physics notes (Tier C — the mechanism/why)

*Added 2026-07-17 (biomechanics round). This chapter's maneuver cues were laddered the same day
(TER-2..TER-8b, training round 2); what follows is the **physics under the ladder** — the cues
and the mechanism agree, and future refinements must keep them laddered to it.*

**TER-11 — Pumping is real mechanical work timed to CURVATURE, not a shove down the slope.** Physics
models of pumping (skateboard half-pipe, BMX/roller "energy pumping," and a pump-track bike-rider
model) converge on one picture: a rider gains speed by actively moving the **centre of mass** —
extending to drive the CoM toward the centre of curvature through a trough/transition, and
retracting (going light) over a crest. The ground reaction is always perpendicular to the surface,
so pushing on a straight down-slope adds no forward speed; propulsion comes from doing work against
the *raised normal force through the curved part* (equivalently, conserving angular momentum as the
CoM's radius of curvature shrinks — as the radius drops, speed must rise). Modelled result: pure
pumping through one banked curve produced **Δv ≈ 1.5 m/s with no pedalling**, and the optimal
pattern is a precisely-timed extend/retract that skilled riders match more closely than novices.
Coaching consequences: **(1)** time the push to the curved transition / trough, *not* the top of the
down-slope; **(2)** pumping is muscular work, not "free speed."
- *Tier:* C. Sources: Golembiewski et al. 2023, *The Dynamics of a Bicycle on a Pump Track*
  (Lagrangian two-point-mass bike+rider model;
  [DOI 10.48550/arXiv.2311.07251](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2311.07251)); *Identification of
  Optimal Movement Patterns for Energy Pumping*, Sports 2023
  ([DOI 10.3390/sports11020031](https://doi.org/10.3390/sports11020031)); Kogelbauer et al. 2024,
  *Mechanical optimization of skateboard pumping*, Phys. Rev. Research
  ([DOI 10.1103/PhysRevResearch.6.033132](https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevResearch.6.033132)).
  Softened by Pinkbike's angular-momentum explainer (Tier B/D). **Grounds TER-2 (laddered same day).**

**TER-12 — Once airborne, the CoM is a PROJECTILE: takeoff speed + angle set the landing, and you
cannot change the CoM path in the air.** After the wheels leave the lip only gravity (and small
drag) act on the centre of mass, so it follows a parabola fixed entirely at takeoff:
`range ≈ v²·sin(2θ)/g`, `peak ≈ (v·sinθ)²/2g`. The rider can rotate/move the bike *around* the CoM
(manual, whip, absorb) but cannot add distance or "save" a mis-set speed once committed; real drag
makes the descent slightly steeper than the ascent. Physics-grounded safety content the next round
must ladder: **(1) the approach speed is the decision — commit before the lip**, because it
determines where you land; **(2) match the landing to the trajectory** — landing G-force scales with
the velocity component *perpendicular* to the landing slope, so a downslope matching your descent
angle is soft while landing to flat drives that whole perpendicular component into you (this is the
mechanism under "never jump to flat"); **(3) boost/squash** at the lip changes takeoff angle/speed by
up to ~±15%, which is why the same lip jumps differently for different riders.
- *Tier:* C/B. Sources: projectile-motion jump-design treatments — trailism.com *Bike Jump Design*
  (parabolic trajectories, clothoid lip, G-limits); Stalefish Labs *Building a Better Kicker*
  (Euler-integrated drag trajectory; landing G-force from the perpendicular velocity component);
  classic projectile mechanics. **Grounds TER-7/TER-8 (drops/jumps) — the
  "commit to speed" and "match the landing" rungs.**

**TER-13 — Biomechanics evidence that pumping is a PRIMARY propulsion mode, not a garnish.**
Notational analysis of elite Supercross BMX found riders spend more of a ~40 s lap **"pumping"
(~17 s)** than pedalling (~12–14 s) or jumping (~6–10 s), executing ~30 hip and ~30 knee extensions
and ~20 shoulder abduction/adduction cycles per lap. On flow/gravity terrain, rhythmic whole-body
extension timed to the terrain — not pedalling — carries much of the speed, which is why the pumping
rung sits lowest and most load-bearing in the ladder (TER-1).
- *Tier:* C. Source (via PubMed): Cowell, McGuigan & Cronin 2012, *Movement and skill analysis of
  supercross bicycle motocross*, J. Strength Cond. Res.
  ([DOI 10.1519/JSC.0b013e318234eb22](https://doi.org/10.1519/JSC.0b013e318234eb22)).
