# Climbing & Descending — Coach Corpus

**Maturity: professional** (round 4, 2026-07-18: closed the technical-climbing, off-camber, and
half-laddered-steeps gaps — see `## Gaps`). Climbing technique · descending · steeps ·
gradient-driven weight distribution. Read [`INDEX.md`](INDEX.md) first (corpus rules, tiers,
SAFETY-FIRST PROGRESSION, tacit-layer disclaimer).

Fact IDs are stable and append-only (`CLD-n`). Every skill fact carries **Prereq / Gear /
Progression**. This chapter contains the corpus's canonical **⚠ DISPUTED** item (the "get your
weight back" descent cue — CLD-4/CLD-5) — read it as the model for how the corpus handles genuine
coaching disagreement.

## Climbing

**CLD-1 — Climbing: stay SEATED, shift weight FORWARD, keep both wheels loaded.** On a steep climb,
weight must stay centred over the bike with load on *both* wheels: too far back and the front
wanders or lifts; too far forward and the rear loses traction and spins. Stay seated (to keep
weight on the rear for drive traction) but sit toward the **nose of the saddle** with the torso
leaning forward and **chest low toward the bars** ("chin over stem" / "chest over bars") to keep
the front wheel pinned. Pull the elbows *straight back* (not down) to add downward pressure on the
front. Eyes up, scanning the line ahead (you don't need to look as far as descending, but do read
the line).
- *Prereq:* FND-1, FND-4, FND-6.
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Progression:* practise the seated-forward, chest-low posture on a mild climb → progress to
  steeper pitches, adjusting how far forward as the gradient grows → add technical features later.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: coachlevi.com *How to Climb a Steep Hill* (centred, seated + lean
  forward, elbows back, eyes up); Liv Cycling (*"boobs over bars"* / chest forward-down, hips
  forward in saddle, chin over stem); REI Climbs & Descents.

**CLD-2 — Shift gears BEFORE the climb; keep a steady cadence, soft-pedal the shift.** It's very
hard to shift under the load of a steep climb (near-impossible on the front, hard on the rear), so
pick the right (easier) gear at the *bottom* before the pitch. Mid-climb, if you must shift, take a
couple of power strokes, briefly *soft-pedal* to unload the drivetrain for the shift, then put the
power back down. Maintain a smooth, steady cadence rather than surging.
- *Prereq:* CLD-1.
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Progression:* practise pre-shifting on rolling terrain until reading the gear for a pitch is
  automatic.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: coachlevi.com (shift before the hill, power→soft-pedal→shift→power);
  Liv Cycling (steady cadence, lighten the stroke to shift).

## Descending — the base

**CLD-3 — Descending base position is CENTRED and standing, NOT "sat right back" — for most
descents.** Stand on level pedals (3 & 9 o'clock), knees bent (not locked), **elbows bent and
pointing outward** (not tucked, not rigid), hips low and centred over the bottom bracket, head up
looking ahead. Weight distributed roughly *evenly* between the wheels. Standing lets the legs act
as suspension and gives the range of motion to shift as terrain changes. Drop the saddle first
(FND-3). This centred base is where you spend most descending time and gives the most options
(shift forward or back as needed). *The "get your weight way back" instinct is wrong for most
descents — see CLD-4/CLD-5.*
- *Prereq:* FND-1, FND-2, FND-4, FND-5.
- *Gear:* helmet; dropper post; knee pads reasonable as terrain steepens.
- *Progression:* find the centred standing base on a mellow descent → hold it while the trail rolls
  and bends → build steepness gradually.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: promountainbike *Descending Technique* (neutral attack base, cranks
  level, centred over BB, *"weight too far back unloads the front wheel"*); Liv Cycling (drop heels,
  bent limbs, chin over stem, small hip shifts); bicyclenest.com.

## ⚠ DISPUTED — "get your weight back" on descents

**CLD-4 — ⚠ DISPUTED: the classic "shift your weight back / butt behind the saddle" descent cue.**
Reputable sources genuinely disagree, so the corpus presents **both** positions with the terrain
each applies to — it does NOT collapse them into one cue.

- **Position A (traditional, still widely taught):** on descents, shift your weight back — on steep
  drop-offs, get your butt behind the saddle, body low, elbows bent, so you don't go over the bars.
  *Tier B.* Source: REI Climbs & Descents (*"position yourself far back so that your butt is
  practically suspended over the rear tire"*; on drop-offs *"lean way back, with your butt behind
  your saddle"*).
- **Position B (modern skills coaching, the correction):** for **most** descents, *centred* is
  correct (CLD-3). "Weight back" is right **only** on genuinely steep terrain, and even then the
  better mental model is *the bike moves forward underneath you* rather than *you move back*. Blanket
  weight-back on moderate terrain unloads the front wheel, kills steering grip, and puts the rider
  at the end of their range of motion — the "flying catapult" (BRK chapter): if the front then
  drops, they're launched forward. *Tier B-consensus.* Source: promountainbike (*"'get your weight
  back' … is only correct on extremely steep terrain"*); evo/Fluidride (*"'get your butt back for a
  steep descent' is a very dangerous one"*); BetterRide (the weight-back critique + "flying
  catapult").

**How the corpus resolves it (SAFETY-FIRST):** teach the **gradient-scaled** model (CLD-5), which
both camps effectively agree on once "steep" is separated from "moderate." The disagreement is
mostly about *when* weight-back applies — and getting a beginner to sit way back on moderate
terrain is the error the modern coaches are correcting. **Do not teach blanket weight-back.**

**CLD-5 — GRADIENT-SCALED weight: centred on moderate; bike-tips-forward on steeps; forward on
techy-flat.** Match position to the actual gradient, shifting *before* you arrive (vision, FND-6),
not reacting:
- **Moderate / trail gradient:** weight **centred**, hips over the BB, wheels roughly evenly
  loaded (CLD-3). Most of your descending.
- **Steep (≈30°+ chutes):** hips move **behind** the BB — how far depends on how steep; on
  genuine cliff-face steeps, hips can be behind the rear axle. The refined model: approach with
  knees moving **forward toward the "horizon line"** (the lip of the descent) to *create elbow
  bend*, then **relax and let the bike roll forward under you** — your mass stays over the BB while
  the bike tips down ahead of you. *"Flexion creates extension":* the forward knee move at the top
  is what lets the arms extend smoothly into the steep without being yanked forward. Each
  rock/roll/undulation brings you back toward the neutral centred position.
- **Technical flat or slight uphill mid-descent:** weight slightly **forward**, elbows driving
  forward — you need front grip to steer/control speed there; sitting back loses it exactly when
  you need it.
- *Prereq:* CLD-3, BRK-2/3 (modulation to control speed while learning the position).
- *Gear:* helmet, dropper, knee pads; full-face + more protection as steeps get real/consequential.
- *Progression:* master the centred base (CLD-3) → practise a short **steep roll** (arms extend
  once, back to neutral at the bottom) on a low-consequence pitch → build steepness only as the
  forward-approach / relax-and-extend move becomes reliable.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: promountainbike (the three gradient cases); evo/Fluidride (horizon
  line, knees-forward, "bike moves forward under you," flexion-creates-extension, "throne position"
  neutral); bicyclenest.com (steeper → more rearward shift). Supersedes the blanket reading of A in
  CLD-4 for teaching purposes, while preserving A's validity on true steeps.

## Descending — braking & control on the way down

**CLD-6 — On descents: FEATHER both brakes, favour the rear as it gets steeper, brake in straight
lines, cover the levers.** This is the braking chapter applied under gravity: modulate (don't grab
— BRK-3); use both brakes but bias toward the **rear** on steep terrain because heavy front braking
there pitches you toward an endo — *without abandoning the front*, which still provides most
stopping power as weight transfers onto it; brake on straight, grippy sections and coast the rough
ones (BRK-4/7); keep fingers covering the levers (BRK-6). One useful teaching ratio (evo/Fluidride):
the front gives ~70% of stopping power but is squeezed only ~30% as hard as the rear.
- *Prereq:* BRK-1..7, CLD-3.
- *Gear:* helmet, dropper; protection scaled to gradient.
- *Progression:* learn braking control on a mellow descent you can take slowly and in control
  first — "learning to brake down steep descents lets you *feel* you're in control, because you
  are" — then add gradient/speed.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: promountainbike (feather, favour rear on steeps but keep front,
  straight-line braking); evo/Fluidride (both brakes, ~70%/30%, dimmer not switch, brake-control
  drill on a known hill); REI (rely more on rear on drop-offs).

**CLD-7 — Pedals level at 3 & 9 (the "platform") whenever you're not pedalling.** Unless actively
pedalling, keep the cranks level so a pedal doesn't clip a rock or stump, and so both feet share the
weighting/bracing job. (Cornering is the exception — outside foot down, COR-3.)
- *Prereq:* FND-1, FND-5.
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: REI Climbs & Descents (level pedals / 3 & 9 platform);
  promountainbike (pedals level except in corners).

## Technical climbing (round 4, 2026-07-18)

*Closes the "technical climbing" L2 gap: ledges/steps, loose-surface traction management, and
switchback climbs — the three technical-climbing sub-skills the chapter previously lacked.*

**CLD-11 — Ledges/steps: a THREE-PHASE weight shift, not a static climbing position — approach in a
bigger gear, weight BACK to let the front wheel "float" up, then weight FORWARD the instant the rear
wheel contacts.** A discrete ledge/rock-step is a distinct sub-skill from steady climbing (CLD-1):
(1) approach with a smooth line and a **bigger gear** than you'd otherwise pick — the extra torque
and resistance gives something to balance against and helps keep rear-wheel traction; (2) as the
front wheel reaches the ledge, weight shifts back and the front wheel is allowed to almost "float"
onto the step (pulling up on the bars assists it) — hitting it hard kills momentum; (3) the instant
the **rear** wheel contacts the ledge, weight shifts forward immediately (or you loop out) while
easing power on to feel whether the tyre is going to spin. On steep ledges, staying seated isn't an
option — stand and balance dynamically. This is the front-wheel-lift skill (`terrain-features.md`
TER-3) applied under load, on a climb, with a second, forward weight shift added for the rear wheel.
- *Prereq:* CLD-1, `terrain-features.md` TER-3 (front wheel lift).
- *Gear:* helmet; standing pedalling comfort is a prerequisite skill in its own right.
- *Progression:* practise the lift/float/weight-forward sequence on a low curb or single brick first
  (Loam Wolf/Drew Bros' progression: basic curb → bigger obstacles → trail-realistic combos) → apply
  to a real rock ledge → a no-dab "trials section" test (Red Bull/Milway) to find where grip actually
  runs out.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: Australian Mountain Bike (Jared Rando, *How to climb rock ledges* —
  the 5-step gear/float/weight-forward sequence); Red Bull (Alan Milway, Gee Atherton's coach —
  technical-climbing section: centred+easy-gear+look-ahead, and testing grip with a no-dab trials
  section); Lee Likes Bikes MTB School (Lee McCormack) — public progression list confirms the same
  skill order (*pedalling up a steep slope → pedalling up a vertical ledge → dynamic traction for
  loose climbs*); the detailed how-to text past the intro is **member-walled**, so only the
  progression's existence and order is cited here, not its technique detail.

**CLD-12 — Loose-surface climbing traction: stay SEATED, keep power SMOOTH (never a punchy stomp),
and pick a gear/cadence that trades torque for tyre-friendliness.** Distinct from the ledge skill
(CLD-11): on a *continuously* loose surface (gravel, loose dirt, sand) rather than a discrete step,
standing unweights the rear tyre and breaks it loose, so the base position is **seated**, sliding
forward on the saddle only as far as needed to keep the front wheel tracking without giving up rear
weight. Power delivery must be round and smooth — a single hard pedal spike is what breaks
traction, not sustained effort — which in practice means a slightly higher cadence and a slightly
easier gear than the same climb on hardpack, not a harder one. If the tyre starts to break loose,
easing off fractionally recovers grip faster than pushing through. This is a genuinely different
prescription from CLD-11's ledge technique (which explicitly calls for standing and a *bigger*
gear) — the two don't contradict; they answer different terrain (a discrete obstacle needing
weight-shift range of motion vs. a continuous surface needing undisturbed rear-tyre load).
- *Prereq:* CLD-1.
- *Gear:* helmet; wider/lower-pressure tyres common for loose terrain (equipment context, not a
  coaching cue).
- *Progression:* isolate the smooth-pedal-stroke drill on a mellow loose section first, focusing on
  cadence and stroke roundness alone → add gradient → add line choice (aim for the firmest-looking
  dirt, not the shortest path).
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: Mountain Bike Action Magazine (McCoy, *How To Ride Loose Climbs* —
  weight over the rear wheel, higher cadence/harder-gear-avoidance, read the surface, look ahead);
  Merlin Cycles (Benji Haworth) — ~90 rpm cadence for traction, slide-forward-on-the-nose on steep
  loose pitches, smooth "bar tugging" timed to pedal power peaks; Red Bull (Alan Milway) — the
  technical-climbing trials-section test as the diagnostic for where a rider is losing grip.

**CLD-13 — Uphill switchbacks: go WIDE on entry to manufacture turn radius, pick a far EXIT target
(not the apex), and pre-shift/pre-position before the turn, not in it.** A tight uphill switchback
fails for a structural reason, not a fitness one: two wheels must independently complete a
180°-ish turn on a narrow trail. The fix is line-based: enter as wide as the trail allows (front
wheel toward the outside edge on entry) so the rear wheel has room to follow a different, easier
arc; look through the corner to a point 6–12 ft **past** the apex, not at the apex itself (staring
at the apex is a common-error trigger — the bike goes where the eyes go, FND-6); complete any gear
shift *before* the turn tightens, since mid-corner shifts under load bog down or skip. Body
position drops low through the tightest part (chest toward the bars, outside/downhill foot loaded
when coasting) then returns upright on exit. A light drag of the **rear** brake (never the front)
is an accepted exception to the general "finish braking before the corner" rule (BRK-4) specifically
for slow-speed switchback speed control — it keeps the front wheel free to track the chosen line
without upsetting balance, which a front-brake tap would.
- *Prereq:* CLD-1, FND-6 (vision), the low-speed balance underlying COR's tight-corner family.
- *Gear:* none specific; practise on a mellow, open switchback or a flat-ground figure-8 first.
- *Progression:* flat-ground figure-8 slow-turn drill (both directions — most riders have a weaker
  side) → apply to a real, mellow uphill switchback → add steepness/looseness only once the wide-
  entry/exit-target pattern is automatic.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: Onward MTB Coaching (Rory Bingham) / syndicated at Spoke Magazine —
  the wide-entry, exit-target, pre-shift, rear-brake-drag-for-speed-control, and figure-8 drill
  content is identical across both, indicating a single named coaching source; Brave Endeavors MTB
  Coaching — independently corroborates go-wide and the eyes-on-exit-target cue ("go wider than you
  think"; "eyes on the prize"), adding the chin/outside-elbow lead cue for initiating the body's
  rotation through the turn.

## Off-camber traverses (round 4, 2026-07-18)

**CLD-14 — Off-camber base position: LOW ready stance, weight centred (not dropped straight back),
outside/downhill foot weighted, bike leaned into the slope MORE than the body.** Off-camber terrain
(the trail surface tilts sideways, away from the direction of travel) narrows the tyre's usable
contact patch and removes the built-in support a berm or flat corner gives — small disruptions cost
grip fast. The base position: drop into the low ready position by bending down at the knees, **not**
by moving weight back (moving back unweights the front tyre and it washes downhill — the same
error mechanism as CLD-3/CLD-4's descending mistake, applied sideways instead of fore-aft); weight
hips over the bottom bracket; load the **outside** (downhill) pedal, which raises the inside pedal
for clearance and helps drive the tyres straight down into the slope rather than at an angle to it.
Lean the *bike* into the (uphill side of the) slope to dig the tyres' edge knobs in, but keep the
*body* more upright than the bike — counter-balancing hips slightly away from the bike keeps weight
centred over the contact patch rather than following the bike's lean. Leaning too far risks sliding
out at the tyre's edge; not leaning enough lets the bike slip down the camber.
- *Prereq:* CLD-3 (the centred-base principle), FND-4.
- *Gear:* helmet; knee pads reasonable as camber/consequence increases.
- *Progression:* build up gradually — start on shallow, low-consequence cambers, not steep
  root-infested ones; practise the low-ready/outside-foot position on flat ground first (open
  concrete or a mellow slope), then apply to real off-camber sections, then add steepness.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: REI Expert Advice (*Mountain Biking: How to Ride Off-Camber* —
  brake-before, chest-over-bar weight-centring, lean + counter-balance hips, pedal position);
  BikeRadar (*How to clamber across cambers* — lean bike+body into the slope without over-leaning,
  weight in the middle, outside foot loaded, build up gradually rather than starting on 30° slopes);
  Singletracks (Jeremiah Stone, Ninja Mountain Bike Performance, professional instructor — low ready
  position via bending down not back, hips over BB, outside pedal drives straight down into the
  slope).

**CLD-15 — Off-camber line choice: finish braking BEFORE the section, stay HIGH on entry, hit
roots/rocks SQUARE, and bail to the UPHILL side if you must dab.** Traction is already at a premium
on a camber, so any braking mid-section is disproportionately likely to break a wheel loose and send
it sliding downhill — all speed adjustment happens before entry (the off-camber analog of BRK-4's
"brake in a straight line, before the corner"). Line choice: start as **high** on the camber as the
trail allows, because the natural drift during the section is downhill — a high entry buys margin
before that drift becomes a problem, and if disrupted, a rider who started high can still make the
exit. Where roots or rocks cross the camber, aim to hit them **square-on** rather than at an angle,
since an angled hit lets them redirect the tyre sideways, which on a camber means downhill. If a dab
becomes necessary, put the foot down on the **uphill** side — dabbing downhill risks the bike
sliding onto the planted foot/leg.
- *Prereq:* CLD-14, BRK-4 (straight-line braking).
- *Gear:* as CLD-14.
- *Progression:* same build-up as CLD-14 — this is the line-choice half of the same skill, taught
  together in practice.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: REI Expert Advice (brake before, find the line on the
  least-pitched part, hit roots straight-on); MBR (Andrew Shandro, former DH pro, Whistler Summer
  Gravity Camps coach — "stay high" as the headline off-camber rule, brake sparingly, a sudden
  application can lock the wheel); Singletracks/Stone (bail on the uphill side if a dab is needed;
  ratchet the pedals on off-camber climbs to avoid a pedal strike without breaking cadence).

## Sustained steeps — the half-laddered gap, closed (round 4, 2026-07-18)

**CLD-16 — The steep-roll → committed-chute progression is a THREE-RUNG ladder, each rung gated on
terrain runout, protection, and a demonstrated "smooth at the bottom" floor — not on confidence
alone.** CLD-5 already describes the *movement* (knees-forward to the horizon line → relax → let
the bike roll forward underneath you); this closes the gap CLD-5 left open — the **ladder** of
terrain a rider should actually be exposed to that movement on, in order:
1. **Rung 1 — the steep roll.** Short, a *known, forgiving runout*, a single arm-extension event
   (down, then back to neutral at the bottom). This is CLD-5's base case. *Gear:* helmet, knee pads.
2. **Rung 2 — a longer steep pitch with undulations.** Rocks/roots/rolls within the descent mean
   *repeated* flexion→extension cycles rather than one — each bump should return the rider toward
   the CLD-3 neutral position before the next one, per CLD-5's "each rock/root/undulation brings you
   back toward neutral." *Gear:* add elbow pads; full-face reasonable as speed/consequence rise.
3. **Rung 3 — consequential/exposed steeps** (cliff-face-style, hips genuinely behind the rear axle
   — CLD-5's most extreme case, and the terrain where CLD-4's Position A is actually correct, per
   CLD-10's pitch-over physics). *Gear:* full-face, body armor; pre-walking/scouting the line before
   riding it is part of the gear/terrain envelope at this rung, not optional.
**The gate between rungs is a floor, not a feeling:** advance only once the current rung is ridden
*smoothly* — no grabbed brakes, no rigid/straight arms at the bottom, a genuine return to neutral —
because the fear response (tense up / retreat rearward, CLD-9's vibration-cost mechanism makes this
literally more costly, not just less controlled) is the same failure mode at every rung, and riding
a rung tense "proves" nothing about readiness for the next one. Never advance a nervous rider
straight from Rung 1 to Rung 3 — the corpus's SAFETY-FIRST rule applied to this specific ladder.
- *Prereq:* CLD-3, CLD-5, BRK-2/3 (braking control — a rider cannot safely commit to Rung 2+ without
  threshold braking already solid).
- *Gear:* escalates by rung, above.
- *Progression:* Rung 1 → Rung 2 → Rung 3, in order, each held until "smooth at the bottom" before
  advancing; regress a rung after a scary or tense run rather than pushing through.
- *Tier:* B-consensus, grounded by the chapter's existing Tier-C physics (CLD-8/9/10 — cross-
  referenced, not re-derived). Source: evo/Fluidride (Simon Lawton) — the short-roll-first
  progression and the "each undulation returns you to neutral" language for longer descents; Red
  Bull (Alan Milway) — "there's no shame in starting small and building up to bigger, gnarlier
  descents"; promountainbike — pre-riding a technical section slowly before committing to speed,
  progressive exposure to terrain just below the rider's limit as the sanctioned way to build steep-
  terrain confidence.

## Extended descents — fatigue and brake-fade craft (round 4, 2026-07-18)

**CLD-17 — On a long/sustained descent, HEAT (mechanical, the brakes') and ARM PUMP (physiological,
the rider's) are two separate failure modes that both get worse the longer braking is continuous —
the coaching answer to both is the same: brake in short, decisive windows, not a continuous drag.**
- **Heat/fade (equipment side, flagged not re-derived):** the mechanic corpus already documents the
  pad-heat mechanism — friction ramps up, peaks in a compound-specific "sweet spot," then falls off
  as the pad surface glazes past that point (`tools/mechanic/brakes.md` BRK-25) — this chapter does
  not re-derive that mechanism, only its coaching consequence: a rider who **drags** the brakes
  continuously down a long descent pushes the pads toward that falloff zone sooner than one who
  brakes in short bursts on the grippiest sections and releases on the rest (CLD-6's "brake on
  straight grippy sections, coast the rough ones" is already the fade-avoidant pattern, not just the
  traction-avoidant one). Bigger rotors and 4-piston calipers exist specifically to buy heat-capacity
  margin on long alpine descents — equipment context for the coach to be aware of, not a technique.
- **Arm pump (rider side):** sustained hard gripping/braking restricts blood flow in the
  fascia-bound forearm compartment — a real physiological limit, not a strength deficit, that gets
  worse the longer the continuous tension lasts and can progress to a clinically-recognised overuse
  condition (Chronic Exertional Compartment Syndrome) in persistent cases. The technique-side
  mitigation is CLD-8's "heavy feet, light hands" applied specifically to grip: drive weight through
  the legs/feet rather than hanging off the bars, keep grip relaxed except when actively modulating,
  and use the calmer stretches of a descent to consciously release grip tension rather than holding
  it constant end to end. A rider whose hands are genuinely "blown" mid-descent should stop and shake
  out rather than continue with a compromised brake grip — weak grip degrades braking control, which
  compounds risk rather than just discomfort.
- *Prereq:* CLD-6, CLD-8, BRK-2/3/6.
- *Gear:* as CLD-6, with the note that descent **duration**, not just steepness, is its own
  escalation axis — build up long descents gradually the same way steepness is built up rung-by-rung
  in CLD-16.
- *Progression:* same progressive-exposure principle as CLD-16 — increase descent length gradually
  rather than jumping straight from short trail laps to a long alpine descent, since both the fade
  and arm-pump failure modes are duration-driven, not just terrain-driven.
- *Tier:* B / B-adjacent-C. Source: Red Bull MTB brake guide (front-brake-primary, squeeze-don't-
  grab, tyre pressure affects braking, 4-piston/metallic-pad upgrades named specifically for "long,
  steep descents"); cross-reference `tools/mechanic/brakes.md` BRK-25 for the pad-heat mechanism
  (not duplicated here — see the mechanic corpus's own citation); ENDURO Mountainbike Magazine,
  quoting Kirsty Sinclair (clinician/owner, Tweed Valley Physiotherapy) on the arm-pump mechanism
  (forearm fascia/blood-flow restriction, the CECS clinical term, and the need to build up ride
  duration/intensity gradually rather than jumping straight into long descents the body hasn't
  adapted to).

## PROGRESSIONS (the organizing principle)

### The ladder (what depends on what)
- **Both climbing and descending are FND-4 (centred + shift) applied to a gradient** — the base is
  balance and the gradient-driven shift. A rider without a solid centred base can't safely add
  steepness.
- **Descending gates on braking control (BRK):** you cannot commit to a steep roll safely without
  threshold braking (BRK-3) and feathering (BRK-2). Fix braking before steeps.
- **Vision (FND-6) drives the *timing*:** you must read gradient changes far enough ahead to shift
  *before* arriving (CLD-5), not react — the same principle drives switchback line choice (CLD-13's
  exit target, not the apex) and off-camber entry height (CLD-15).
- **Steep-descent order is now an explicit rung ladder (CLD-16):** centred base (CLD-3) → short
  steep roll on low consequence (Rung 1) → longer steeps with undulations (Rung 2) →
  consequential/exposed steeps (Rung 3), each gated on a "smooth at the bottom" floor, not
  confidence, with gear/protection escalating per rung. Never jump a nervous rider straight onto a
  consequential chute — the fear response (tense up / retreat rearward) is itself the danger
  (evo/Fluidride), and CLD-9's vibration-cost physics shows it is literally more costly, not just
  less controlled.
- **Technical climbing (CLD-11/12) forks on terrain type, not skill level:** a discrete ledge/step
  needs standing + a bigger gear + a two-phase weight shift (back-then-forward); a continuously
  loose surface needs the opposite — staying seated + a smoother/lighter pedal stroke. Diagnose which
  terrain type is in front of the rider before prescribing a fix — applying the ledge fix to a loose
  climb (or vice versa) makes it worse.
- **Descent duration is its own axis, independent of steepness (CLD-17):** heat/fade and arm pump
  are both duration-driven failure modes — build up long descents gradually the same way steepness
  is built up rung-by-rung, and don't assume a rider who's solid on short steep rolls is automatically
  ready for a long alpine descent at the same gradient.

### Common errors → corrections
- **Blanket "weight back" on moderate descents** → centred base; unloading the front loses steering
  (CLD-3/4/5). The corpus's headline correction.
- **Approaching a steep already sat way back, arms straight ("end of the rope")** → move forward at
  the *knees* toward the horizon line to create elbow bend, then relax and let the bike roll forward
  (CLD-5). Straight arms at the top = getting yanked forward = the fear cycle.
- **Tensing up / gripping hard from fear** → relax; tension removes the arms-as-suspension function
  and forces the retreat-rearward instinct (evo/Fluidride, FND-2).
- **Front-wheel lift / rear-wheel skip on climbs** → too far back (front lifts) or too far forward
  (rear breaks traction); find the centred-but-forward climbing balance (CLD-1).
- **Grabbing the front brake on a steep** → feather, favour rear, keep front modulated (CLD-6/BRK-3).
- **Rear wheel spins/loops out on a ledge** → weight arrived forward too early (before the rear
  wheel actually contacted the step) or too late; the sequence is back-then-forward, timed to the
  rear wheel's contact, not the front's (CLD-11).
- **Standing and mashing on a continuously loose climb** → unweights the rear tyre exactly where it
  needs load; sit, smooth the stroke, ease off fractionally rather than pushing through a slip
  (CLD-12).
- **Staring at the switchback apex, stalling mid-turn** → look 6–12 ft past the apex at the exit
  target; the bike (and the rider's turning) follows the eyes (CLD-13, FND-6).
- **Dropping straight back into an off-camber section** → unweights the front, which then washes
  downhill; drop *down* at the knees into the low ready position, not *back* (CLD-14).
- **Braking mid-camber** → already-scarce grip gets split between braking and staying upright; all
  speed adjustment happens before the section (CLD-15/BRK-4).
- **Dragging the brakes continuously down a long descent** → pushes pads toward heat falloff and
  keeps forearms under sustained tension (arm pump) simultaneously; brake in short windows on
  grippy sections, release on the rest — the fade-avoidant pattern is the same pattern as the
  traction-avoidant one (CLD-6/CLD-17).

### How skills couple
- Braking (BRK) ↔ descending (CLD-6): the same modulation skill under load, and the same pattern
  (short windows, not a drag) that also avoids brake fade and arm pump on long descents (CLD-17).
- Vision (FND-6) → gradient reading + line choice on the way down, exit-target line choice on
  switchbacks (CLD-13), and entry-height line choice off-camber (CLD-15).
- Weight shift (FND-4) is the shared engine of climbing, descending, ledges (CLD-11), and off-camber
  (CLD-14) alike — always centred-to-the-terrain-type, never a blanket fore/aft default.
- Steeps + tight turns = switchbacks → CLD-13 here (uphill) and `terrain-features.md` (L2, downhill
  switchbacks), both built on FND-6 + COR-8.
- Terrain-feature skills (`terrain-features.md`) feed technical climbing directly: CLD-11's ledge
  technique is TER-3's front-wheel lift applied under climbing load.

## Physics notes (Tier C — the mechanism/why)

*Added 2026-07-17 (biomechanics round). Grounds the suspension-rider interaction and the gradient/
braking cues — the **why**, not new cues.*

**CLD-8 — The rider IS the primary suspension: ~80–85% of system mass is the body, so limbs-as-
suspension dominate.** Because the rider is far heavier than the bike, actively flexing the arms and
legs to hold the CoM steady while the wheels track the ground ("heavy feet, light hands"; the *bike*
moves under you) isolates the mass from terrain more than the mechanical suspension does. This is the
mechanism under CLD-5's "flexion creates extension" and the ready-position bent-limb requirement:
straight, locked limbs remove the largest available suspension travel and force the retreat-rearward
fear response.
- *Tier:* C. Source: mass-distribution reasoning, corroborated by the two-point-mass rider+bike
  models used in the pumping literature (the rider mass and its controlled link to the bike carry the
  dynamics): Golembiewski et al. 2023
  ([DOI 10.48550/arXiv.2311.07251](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2311.07251)).

**CLD-9 — Terrain vibration has a measurable energetic cost, which staying loose reduces.** Off-road
descending raises heart rate and oxygen cost above road descending at matched (near-zero) power
output, and the difference tracks terrain-induced vibration transmitted between bike and rider; the
study recommends skills + equipment that *reduce* transmitted vibration. Grounds two cues with
mechanism: **(1)** tensing up (a CLD error) is literally costly — a rigid rider transmits and must
damp more vibration and fatigues faster; **(2)** using the body as suspension and choosing smoother
lines is energy economy, not only control.
- *Tier:* C. Source (via PubMed): Miller, Macdermid, Fink & Stannard 2016, *Performance and
  physiological effects of different descending strategies for cross-country mountain biking*,
  Eur. J. Sport Sci. ([DOI 10.1080/17461391.2016.1237550](https://doi.org/10.1080/17461391.2016.1237550)).

**CLD-10 — Weight-transfer physics grounds "favour the rear on steeps" AND the kernel of the ⚠
DISPUTE.** On a descent the static load is already shifted forward by the gradient, so the front tyre
sits nearer its pitch-over limit before you even brake: the critical deceleration `a_crit ≈ g·(b/h)`
(`b` = CoG-to-front-contact distance, `h` = CoG height; see `braking-traction.md` BRK-9) is reached
sooner — which is *why* CLD-6 biases toward the rear as it steepens (without abandoning the front,
which still carries most load). The same equation explains the grain of truth in **Position A** of
CLD-4: moving the CoM down and rearward increases `b` and decreases `h`, genuinely raising `a_crit`,
so a rearward shift *does* buy pitch-over margin on true steeps. It does **not** license blanket
weight-back on moderate terrain (**Position B**), where it needlessly unloads the front and kills
steering grip. Physics informs the envelope of the dispute; it does not collapse it.
- *Tier:* C. Sources: Skatulla, Maier & Schmidt 2023
  ([DOI 10.24404/63fe5c46c9d7c3ebc76e944b](https://doi.org/10.24404/63fe5c46c9d7c3ebc76e944b));
  Klug et al. 2017 ([DOI 10.1016/j.ifacol.2017.08.173](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ifacol.2017.08.173));
  Calspan/Cornell bicycle pitch-over study.
