## Climbing

**CLD-1 — Climbing: stay SEATED, shift weight FORWARD, keep both wheels loaded.** On a steep climb,
weight must stay centred over the bike with load on *both* wheels: too far back and the front
wanders or lifts; too far forward and the rear loses traction and spins. Stay seated (to keep
weight on the rear for drive traction) but sit toward the **nose of the saddle** with the torso
leaning forward and **chest low toward the bars** ("chin over stem" / "chest over bars") to keep
the front wheel pinned. Pull the elbows *straight back* (not down) to add downward pressure on the
front. Eyes up, scanning the line ahead (you don't need to look as far as descending, but do read
the line).
- *Prereq:* FND-1, FND-4, FND-6.
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Progression:* practise the seated-forward, chest-low posture on a mild climb → progress to
  steeper pitches, adjusting how far forward as the gradient grows → add technical features later.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: coachlevi.com *How to Climb a Steep Hill* (centred, seated + lean
  forward, elbows back, eyes up); Liv Cycling (*"boobs over bars"* / chest forward-down, hips
  forward in saddle, chin over stem); REI Climbs & Descents.

**CLD-2 — Shift gears BEFORE the climb; keep a steady cadence, soft-pedal the shift.** It's very
hard to shift under the load of a steep climb (near-impossible on the front, hard on the rear), so
pick the right (easier) gear at the *bottom* before the pitch. Mid-climb, if you must shift, take a
couple of power strokes, briefly *soft-pedal* to unload the drivetrain for the shift, then put the
power back down. Maintain a smooth, steady cadence rather than surging.
- *Prereq:* CLD-1.
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Progression:* practise pre-shifting on rolling terrain until reading the gear for a pitch is
  automatic.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: coachlevi.com (shift before the hill, power→soft-pedal→shift→power);
  Liv Cycling (steady cadence, lighten the stroke to shift).

## Descending — the base

**CLD-3 — Descending base position is CENTRED and standing, NOT "sat right back" — for most
descents.** Stand on level pedals (3 & 9 o'clock), knees bent (not locked), **elbows bent and
pointing outward** (not tucked, not rigid), hips low and centred over the bottom bracket, head up
looking ahead. Weight distributed roughly *evenly* between the wheels. Standing lets the legs act
as suspension and gives the range of motion to shift as terrain changes. Drop the saddle first
(FND-3). This centred base is where you spend most descending time and gives the most options
(shift forward or back as needed). *The "get your weight way back" instinct is wrong for most
descents — see CLD-4/CLD-5.*
- *Prereq:* FND-1, FND-2, FND-4, FND-5.
- *Gear:* helmet; dropper post; knee pads reasonable as terrain steepens.
- *Progression:* find the centred standing base on a mellow descent → hold it while the trail rolls
  and bends → build steepness gradually.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: promountainbike *Descending Technique* (neutral attack base, cranks
  level, centred over BB, *"weight too far back unloads the front wheel"*); Liv Cycling (drop heels,
  bent limbs, chin over stem, small hip shifts); bicyclenest.com.

## ⚠ DISPUTED — "get your weight back" on descents

**CLD-4 — ⚠ DISPUTED: the classic "shift your weight back / butt behind the saddle" descent cue.**
Reputable sources genuinely disagree, so the corpus presents **both** positions with the terrain
each applies to — it does NOT collapse them into one cue.

- **Position A (traditional, still widely taught):** on descents, shift your weight back — on steep
  drop-offs, get your butt behind the saddle, body low, elbows bent, so you don't go over the bars.
  *Tier B.* Source: REI Climbs & Descents (*"position yourself far back so that your butt is
  practically suspended over the rear tire"*; on drop-offs *"lean way back, with your butt behind
  your saddle"*).
- **Position B (modern skills coaching, the correction):** for **most** descents, *centred* is
  correct (CLD-3). "Weight back" is right **only** on genuinely steep terrain, and even then the
  better mental model is *the bike moves forward underneath you* rather than *you move back*. Blanket
  weight-back on moderate terrain unloads the front wheel, kills steering grip, and puts the rider
  at the end of their range of motion — the "flying catapult" (BRK chapter): if the front then
  drops, they're launched forward. *Tier B-consensus.* Source: promountainbike (*"'get your weight
  back' … is only correct on extremely steep terrain"*); evo/Fluidride (*"'get your butt back for a
  steep descent' is a very dangerous one"*); BetterRide (the weight-back critique + "flying
  catapult").

**How the corpus resolves it (SAFETY-FIRST):** teach the **gradient-scaled** model (CLD-5), which
both camps effectively agree on once "steep" is separated from "moderate." The disagreement is
mostly about *when* weight-back applies — and getting a beginner to sit way back on moderate
terrain is the error the modern coaches are correcting. **Do not teach blanket weight-back.**

**CLD-5 — GRADIENT-SCALED weight: centred on moderate; bike-tips-forward on steeps; forward on
techy-flat.** Match position to the actual gradient, shifting *before* you arrive (vision, FND-6),
not reacting:
- **Moderate / trail gradient:** weight **centred**, hips over the BB, wheels roughly evenly
  loaded (CLD-3). Most of your descending.
- **Steep (≈30°+ chutes):** hips move **behind** the BB — how far depends on how steep; on
  genuine cliff-face steeps, hips can be behind the rear axle. The refined model: approach with
  knees moving **forward toward the "horizon line"** (the lip of the descent) to *create elbow
  bend*, then **relax and let the bike roll forward under you** — your mass stays over the BB while
  the bike tips down ahead of you. *"Flexion creates extension":* the forward knee move at the top
  is what lets the arms extend smoothly into the steep without being yanked forward. Each
  rock/roll/undulation brings you back toward the neutral centred position.
- **Technical flat or slight uphill mid-descent:** weight slightly **forward**, elbows driving
  forward — you need front grip to steer/control speed there; sitting back loses it exactly when
  you need it.
- *Prereq:* CLD-3, BRK-2/3 (modulation to control speed while learning the position).
- *Gear:* helmet, dropper, knee pads; full-face + more protection as steeps get real/consequential.
- *Progression:* master the centred base (CLD-3) → practise a short **steep roll** (arms extend
  once, back to neutral at the bottom) on a low-consequence pitch → build steepness only as the
  forward-approach / relax-and-extend move becomes reliable.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: promountainbike (the three gradient cases); evo/Fluidride (horizon
  line, knees-forward, "bike moves forward under you," flexion-creates-extension, "throne position"
  neutral); bicyclenest.com (steeper → more rearward shift). Supersedes the blanket reading of A in
  CLD-4 for teaching purposes, while preserving A's validity on true steeps.

## Descending — braking & control on the way down

**CLD-6 — On descents: FEATHER both brakes, favour the rear as it gets steeper, brake in straight
lines, cover the levers.** This is the braking chapter applied under gravity: modulate (don't grab
— BRK-3); use both brakes but bias toward the **rear** on steep terrain because heavy front braking
there pitches you toward an endo — *without abandoning the front*, which still provides most
stopping power as weight transfers onto it; brake on straight, grippy sections and coast the rough
ones (BRK-4/7); keep fingers covering the levers (BRK-6). One useful teaching ratio (evo/Fluidride):
the front gives ~70% of stopping power but is squeezed only ~30% as hard as the rear.
- *Prereq:* BRK-1..7, CLD-3.
- *Gear:* helmet, dropper; protection scaled to gradient.
- *Progression:* learn braking control on a mellow descent you can take slowly and in control
  first — "learning to brake down steep descents lets you *feel* you're in control, because you
  are" — then add gradient/speed.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: promountainbike (feather, favour rear on steeps but keep front,
  straight-line braking); evo/Fluidride (both brakes, ~70%/30%, dimmer not switch, brake-control
  drill on a known hill); REI (rely more on rear on drop-offs).

**CLD-7 — Pedals level at 3 & 9 (the "platform") whenever you're not pedalling.** Unless actively
pedalling, keep the cranks level so a pedal doesn't clip a rock or stump, and so both feet share the
weighting/bracing job. (Cornering is the exception — outside foot down, COR-3.)
- *Prereq:* FND-1, FND-5.
- *Gear:* helmet.
- *Tier:* B-consensus. Source: REI Climbs & Descents (level pedals / 3 & 9 platform);
  promountainbike (pedals level except in corners).

## PROGRESSIONS (the organizing principle)

### The ladder (what depends on what)
- **Both climbing and descending are FND-4 (centred + shift) applied to a gradient** — the base is
  balance and the gradient-driven shift. A rider without a solid centred base can't safely add
  steepness.
- **Descending gates on braking control (BRK):** you cannot commit to a steep roll safely without
  threshold braking (BRK-3) and feathering (BRK-2). Fix braking before steeps.
- **Vision (FND-6) drives the *timing*:** you must read gradient changes far enough ahead to shift
  *before* arriving (CLD-5), not react.
- **Steep-descent order:** centred base (CLD-3) → short steep roll on low consequence → longer
  steeps → consequential/exposed steeps (with gear escalation). Never jump a nervous rider straight
  onto a consequential chute — the fear response (tense up / retreat rearward) is itself the danger
  (evo/Fluidride).

### Common errors → corrections
- **Blanket "weight back" on moderate descents** → centred base; unloading the front loses steering
  (CLD-3/4/5). The corpus's headline correction.
- **Approaching a steep already sat way back, arms straight ("end of the rope")** → move forward at
  the *knees* toward the horizon line to create elbow bend, then relax and let the bike roll forward
  (CLD-5). Straight arms at the top = getting yanked forward = the fear cycle.
- **Tensing up / gripping hard from fear** → relax; tension removes the arms-as-suspension function
  and forces the retreat-rearward instinct (evo/Fluidride, FND-2).
- **Front-wheel lift / rear-wheel skip on climbs** → too far back (front lifts) or too far forward
  (rear breaks traction); find the centred-but-forward climbing balance (CLD-1).
- **Grabbing the front brake on a steep** → feather, favour rear, keep front modulated (CLD-6/BRK-3).

### How skills couple
- Braking (BRK) ↔ descending (CLD-6): the same modulation skill under load.
- Vision (FND-6) → gradient reading + line choice on the way down.
- Weight shift (FND-4) is the shared engine of both climbing and descending.
- Steeps + tight turns = switchbacks → `terrain-features.md` (L2), built on CLD-5 + COR-8.
